2. Methodology
To address the critical challenge of accurately predicting railway
track settlement induced by adjacent deep excavations, this study pro­
poses an LLM-enhanced GNN framework for quantile prediction under
uncertainty. As illustrated in Fig. 1, the proposed framework marks a
substantial methodological innovation by unifying unstructured textual
narratives from construction records with spatiotemporal sensor data.
Unlike conventional models that rely solely on numerical inputs, the
proposed framework leverages transformer-based LLMs to embed
domain-specific semantic context, thereby enabling semantically
enriched feature representations and cross-modal fusion. The overall
architecture comprises four core components, each described in detail
below.
(1) Data collection: During the excavation process, comprehensive
monitoring logs from surrounding stations are systematically collected,
encompassing two distinct data modalities: unstructured textual records
and structured time-series measurements. The textual data—originally
documented in Chinese—comprises a range of contextual attributes,
such as timestamps, rainfall levels, construction conditions, soil classi­
fications, and expert observations or conclusions. Concurrently, the
time-series modality captures sequential settlement measurements
recorded at multiple monitoring points over predefined intervals, of­
fering high-resolution insights into temporal deformation patterns.
(2) Text preprocessing: The raw textual records, originally composed
in Chinese, are first translated into English using a predefined Chine­
se–English lexicon to preserve semantic integrity. These translated
components are then structured into coherent, human-readable narra­
tives using a standardized templating scheme to ensure consistency and
interpretability. The resulting textual sequences are subsequently toke­
nized and embedded into fixed-dimensional vector representations via a
pretrained LLM, thereby enabling seamless integration with comple­
mentary data modalities in the downstream multimodal learning
pipeline.
(3) Model construction: The proposed GTAF model extracts spatio­
temporal features from both text and time-series data using Graph
WaveNet, a Text Transformer, and an attention-based fusion. The Graph
WaveNet architecture is utilized to dynamically learn a self-adaptive
graph structure from time-series vectors, thereby capturing complex
spatial dependencies among monitoring locations. In parallel, a Text
Transformer model is employed to extract temporal patterns and se­
mantic cues embedded within the textual vector representations. These
modality-specific features are subsequently integrated through an
attention-based fusion mechanism, which adaptively emphasizes salient
signals across both modalities to construct a coherent and contextually
enriched multimodal representation.
(4) Quantile prediction: To explicitly capture predictive uncertainty,
the integrated spatiotemporal representations are reshaped through
extensile quantile estimation. The GTAF model is optimized using a
quantile loss function, which facilitates the generation of prediction
intervals at multiple confidence levels. This probabilistic output sup­
ports the robust estimation of track settlement at various monitoring
locations, effectively accounting for inherent data variability and in­
consistencies arising from cross-modal integration.

2.1. Text tokenization
This section focuses solely on explaining the text tokenization pro­
cess, with the remaining components of text preprocessing addressed in
Section 3.3 and further exemplified in the case study. Herein, unstruc­
tured textual inputs are semantically encoded into fixed-dimensional
embeddings using a pretrained LLM, thereby enabling meaningful rep­
resentation of linguistic information. These embeddings, together with
the corresponding time-series data, are subsequently fed into the pro­
posed GTAF model. Fig. 2 illustrates this process using an example
sentence, “The soil type remained undisturbed.” The process comprises
three principal steps, detailed as follows:
(1) Tokenization: The input text is split into tokens, such as words,
subwords, or characters, depending on the tokenization scheme
employed by LLM. Special tokens (e.g., ’G˙ ’) are introduced where
necessary, and padding tokens (e.g., ‘<|endoftext|>’) are
included to match the required sequence length across inputs.
These tokens are then mapped to their corresponding token IDs
and paired with an attention mask, wherein a value of 1 denotes
meaningful content and 0 identifies padding. This attention
mechanism ensures that the model selectively attends to relevant
linguistic information during subsequent stages of representation
learning.
(2) Transformer embedding: The token IDs are first projected into
continuous vector representations via the embedding layer of
LLM, yielding representations that conform to the model’s fixed
dimensional specifications. These embeddings are subsequently
propagated through a series of Transformer layers, wherein
multi-head self-attention mechanisms iteratively capture
contextual dependencies and semantic relationships across the
input sequence, thereby enriching the representational capacity
of the model for downstream multimodal integration.
(3) Representation extraction: In the final stage, the hidden states
generated across successive Transformer layers progressively
encode higher-order abstractions of the input text. The terminal
output of the final layer captures the model’s most refined se­
mantic understanding of the sequence. Ultimately, the hidden
state associated with the final token in the sequence is extracted,
serving as a summary representation of the entire input. This
context-enriched vector, which encapsulates the cumulative se­
mantic and syntactic information, is then employed to inform
downstream prediction tasks within the multimodal framework.
This stage leverages the Transformer architecture as the core
computational framework for processing textual inputs, utilizing
stacked layers of multi-head self-attention and position-wise feed-for­
ward networks to model intricate linguistic dependencies. As illustrated
in Fig. 3, the encoder–decoder configuration underpins the Transformer
design adopted by LLMs [40]. Table 1 outlines the configurations of the
four LLMs analyzed in this study. Differences in tokenization strategies
and vocabulary sizes among these models substantially affect the
resulting token sequences and their corresponding IDs. Notably, GPT-2
and LLaMA adopt decoder-only architectures, BERT employs an
encoder-only design, and T5 integrates both encoder and decoder
components. These architectural distinctions play a critical role in
shaping how each model encodes semantic information and constructs
contextually rich textual representations.

2.2. Graph WaveNet-Transformer-Attention fusion (GTAF) model
Upon deriving vectorized representations from both textual and
time-series modalities, the proposed GTAF model is employed to distill
spatiotemporal features critical for predictive analysis. As illustrated in
Fig. 1, the GTAF architecture comprises three principal components: (1)
Graph WaveNet to model spatial dependencies through a self-adaptive
graph structure; (2) A Text Transformer to capture temporal patterns
embedded within the textual modality; and (3) An attention-based
fusion module to integrate spatial and temporal cues into a unified
representation. This fused representation is subsequently mapped to
probabilistic forecasts via extensile quantile estimation, thereby facili­
tating robust uncertainty quantification across diverse monitoring
points. The subsequent sections offer a comprehensive exposition of
each architectural component and its functional contribution to the
overall predictive framework.
2.2.1. Graph WaveNet
Graph WaveNet, as originally introduced in [42], is designed to
develop self-adaptive graphs capable of capturing spatial dependencies
embedded within long-range time-series data. Fig. 4 illustrates the
modified framework of Graph WaveNet, which consists of two main
components: a gated temporal convolutional network (Gated TCN) for
temporal modeling and a graph convolutional network (GCN) for
capturing spatial correlations. This dual-stream design facilitates the
extraction of complex spatiotemporal patterns essential for accurate
predictive modeling.
The processing pipeline begins with the application of a twodimensional convolution to the time-series vectors, which are then fed
into Gated TCN to capture temporal dependencies. At each layer, the
Gated TCN output is combined with dynamically learned self-adaptive
graphs and passed through a GCN, enabling the model to learn
evolving spatial correlations across monitoring locations. To facilitate
stable training and enhance representational capacity, residual con­
nections are incorporated to preserve gradient flow, while skip con­
nections integrate multi-scale features across successive layers. A final
two-dimensional convolution is applied to refine the aggregated
spatial features. The architectural details of the Gated TCN and GCN
modules are elaborated in the subsequent sections.
(1) Gated TCN.
The Gated TCN module comprises two parallel temporal convolution
layers: TCN-a and TCN-b. TCN-a applies a hyperbolic tangent (tanh)
activation function to extract salient temporal features from the input
sequence, while TCN-b utilizes a sigmoid activation to generate a gating
signal that modulates the information flow. This gating operation de­
termines the extent to which features extracted by TCN-a are retained or
suppressed. The final output is computed through an element-wise gated
fusion of the two pathways, allowing the model to selectively emphasize
temporally informative signals. The mathematical formulation of this
operation is presented as follows:

2.2.2. Text Transformer
Following the tokenization of the LLM, a Text Transformer extracts
low-dimensional temporal features from high-dimensional textual vec­
tors, thereby facilitating feature fusion on a unified scale. As depicted in
Fig. 5, the Text Transformer architecture follows the standard Trans­
former structure [40], incorporating the multi-head attention mecha­
nism while excluding the feed-forward network. This architectural
adjustment is strategically introduced to reduce the risk of overfitting,
particularly in the context of limited text data availability.
As shown in Fig. 5, this module comprises two primary components:
a dimensionality-reducing decoder and a Transformer architecture. The
decoder first compresses the high-dimensional textual embeddings
generated by the LLM into a lower-dimensional latent space, facilitating
efficient processing. Subsequently, the Transformer captures contextual
dependencies across the input sequence. To be more specific, the lowerdimensional textual vectors are passed through the multi-head attention
mechanism, where the scaled dot-product attention computes the output
for each attention head. The outputs from all attention heads are then
concatenated and linearly projected to produce the final output. The

temporal dynamics embedded in the text.
2.2.3. Attention-based fusion
An attention-based fusion module is employed to integrate spatial
and temporal representations by selectively emphasizing the most
salient components. This mechanism captures interdependencies across
modalities, thereby enhancing the model’s capacity to align and prior­
itize critical features during the fusion process.

facilitate cross-modal alignment, the temporal features are first
expanded into three dimensions by uniformly assigning time informa­

2.3. Quantile prediction
The predictive output of the attention-based fusion module is formulated using a quantile-based approach, thereby enabling the esti­
mation of uncertainty within the forecasting process. To optimize the
GTAF model performance and facilitate rigorous evaluation, quantile
loss is employed in conjunction with standard performance metrics. The
selected quantiles delineate the lower and upper bounds of the predic­
tive distribution. For instance, the 0.05 and 0.95 quantiles correspond to
a 90 % confidence interval. This formulation allows the model to
characterize the inherent uncertainty and variability of future outcomes,
offering a probabilistic perspective essential for robust temporal
prediction.
(1) Quantile loss.
To quantify uncertainty in settlement predictions, the GTAF model
employs a quantile loss framework that estimates prediction intervals
through the specification of multiple quantiles. This approach enables
the model to characterize the distributional properties of the output
rather than relying on point estimates alone. For a predefined set of
quantiles Q = (q1, q2, ⋯, qM), the corresponding quantile losses are
computed independently and aggregated to form the overall loss func­
tion. During prediction, the model produces outputs for each specified
quantile, thereby capturing a comprehensive range of plausible out­
comes. The formulation of the total loss function is as follows:

measures the proportion of true values captured within the predicted
intervals; Prediction Interval Normalized Average Width (PINAW),
which quantifies the relative width of the intervals; and Coverage Width
Criterion (CWC), which balances coverage and interval tightness. These

The three metrics jointly assess the quality of probabilistic forecasts
by capturing the trade-off between interval coverage and sharpness. A
higher PICP indicates greater inclusion of observed values falling within
the estimated bounds, while a lower PINAW indicates a narrower pre­
diction interval. The CWC synthesizes these two dimensions into a
unified measure, where lower values indicate prediction intervals that
are simultaneously precise and reliably encompass the true outcomes.
