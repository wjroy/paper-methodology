2. Methodology
To address the critical challenge of accurately predicting railway track settlement induced by adjacent deep excavations, this study proposes an LLM-enhanced GNN framework for quantile prediction under uncertainty. As illustrated in Fig. 1, the proposed framework marks a substantial methodological innovation by unifying unstructured textual narratives from construction records with spatiotemporal sensor data. Unlike conventional models that rely solely on numerical inputs, the proposed framework leverages transformer-based LLMs to embed domain-specific semantic context, thereby enabling semantically enriched feature representations and cross-modal fusion. The overall architecture comprises four core components, each described in detail below.
(1) Data collection: During the excavation process, comprehensive monitoring logs from surrounding stations are systematically collected, encompassing two distinct data modalities: unstructured textual records and structured time-series measurements. The textual data—originally documented in Chinese—comprises a range of contextual attributes, such as timestamps, rainfall levels, construction conditions, soil classifications, and expert observations or conclusions. Concurrently, the time-series modality captures sequential settlement measurements recorded at multiple monitoring points over predefined intervals, offering high-resolution insights into temporal deformation patterns.
(2) Text preprocessing: The raw textual records, originally composed in Chinese, are first translated into English using a predefined Chinese–English lexicon to preserve semantic integrity. These translated components are then structured into coherent, human-readable narratives using a standardized templating scheme to ensure consistency and interpretability. The resulting textual sequences are subsequently tokenized and embedded into fixed-dimensional vector representations via a pretrained LLM, thereby enabling seamless integration with complementary data modalities in the downstream multimodal learning pipeline.
(3) Model construction: The proposed GTAF model extracts spatiotemporal features from both text and time-series data using Graph WaveNet, a Text Transformer, and an attention-based fusion. The Graph WaveNet architecture is utilized to dynamically learn a self-adaptive graph structure from time-series vectors, thereby capturing complex spatial dependencies among monitoring locations. In parallel, a Text Transformer model is employed to extract temporal patterns and semantic cues embedded within the textual vector representations. These modality-specific features are subsequently integrated through an attention-based fusion mechanism, which adaptively emphasizes salient signals across both modalities to construct a coherent and contextually enriched multimodal representation.
(4) Quantile prediction: To explicitly capture predictive uncertainty, the integrated spatiotemporal representations are reshaped through extensile quantile estimation. The GTAF model is optimized using a quantile loss function, which facilitates the generation of prediction intervals at multiple confidence levels. This probabilistic output supports the robust estimation of track settlement at various monitoring locations, effectively accounting for inherent data variability and inconsistencies arising from cross-modal integration.
2.1. Text tokenization
This section focuses solely on explaining the text tokenization process, with the remaining components of text preprocessing addressed in Section 3.3 and further exemplified in the case study. Herein, unstructured textual inputs are semantically encoded into fixed-dimensional embeddings using a pretrained LLM, thereby enabling meaningful representation of linguistic information. These embeddings, together with the corresponding time-series data, are subsequently fed into the proposed GTAF model. Fig. 2 illustrates this process using an example sentence, “The soil type remained undisturbed.” The process comprises three principal steps, detailed as follows:
(1)
Tokenization: The input text is split into tokens, such as words, subwords, or characters, depending on the tokenization scheme employed by LLM. Special tokens (e.g., 'Ġ') are introduced where necessary, and padding tokens (e.g., ‘<|endoftext|>’) are included to match the required sequence length across inputs. These tokens are then mapped to their corresponding token IDs and paired with an attention mask, wherein a value of 1 denotes meaningful content and 0 identifies padding. This attention mechanism ensures that the model selectively attends to relevant linguistic information during subsequent stages of representation learning.
(2)
Transformer embedding: The token IDs are first projected into continuous vector representations via the embedding layer of LLM, yielding representations that conform to the model’s fixed dimensional specifications. These embeddings are subsequently propagated through a series of Transformer layers, wherein multi-head self-attention mechanisms iteratively capture contextual dependencies and semantic relationships across the input sequence, thereby enriching the representational capacity of the model for downstream multimodal integration.
(3)
Representation extraction: In the final stage, the hidden states generated across successive Transformer layers progressively encode higher-order abstractions of the input text. The terminal output of the final layer captures the model’s most refined semantic understanding of the sequence. Ultimately, the hidden state associated with the final token in the sequence is extracted, serving as a summary representation of the entire input. This context-enriched vector, which encapsulates the cumulative semantic and syntactic information, is then employed to inform downstream prediction tasks within the multimodal framework.
This stage leverages the Transformer architecture as the core computational framework for processing textual inputs, utilizing stacked layers of multi-head self-attention and position-wise feed-forward networks to model intricate linguistic dependencies. As illustrated in Fig. 3, the encoder–decoder configuration underpins the Transformer design adopted by LLMs [40]. Table 1 outlines the configurations of the four LLMs analyzed in this study. Differences in tokenization strategies and vocabulary sizes among these models substantially affect the resulting token sequences and their corresponding IDs. Notably, GPT-2 and LLaMA adopt decoder-only architectures, BERT employs an encoder-only design, and T5 integrates both encoder and decoder components. These architectural distinctions play a critical role in shaping how each model encodes semantic information and constructs contextually rich textual representations.
2.2. Graph WaveNet-Transformer-Attention fusion (GTAF) model
Upon deriving vectorized representations from both textual and time-series modalities, the proposed GTAF model is employed to distill spatiotemporal features critical for predictive analysis. As illustrated in Fig. 1, the GTAF architecture comprises three principal components: (1) Graph WaveNet to model spatial dependencies through a self-adaptive graph structure; (2) A Text Transformer to capture temporal patterns embedded within the textual modality; and (3) An attention-based fusion module to integrate spatial and temporal cues into a unified representation. This fused representation is subsequently mapped to probabilistic forecasts via extensile quantile estimation, thereby facilitating robust uncertainty quantification across diverse monitoring points. The subsequent sections offer a comprehensive exposition of each architectural component and its functional contribution to the overall predictive framework.
2.2.1. Graph WaveNet
Graph WaveNet, as originally introduced in [42], is designed to develop self-adaptive graphs capable of capturing spatial dependencies embedded within long-range time-series data. Fig. 4 illustrates the modified framework of Graph WaveNet, which consists of two main components: a gated temporal convolutional network (Gated TCN) for temporal modeling and a graph convolutional network (GCN) for capturing spatial correlations. This dual-stream design facilitates the extraction of complex spatiotemporal patterns essential for accurate predictive modeling.
The processing pipeline begins with the application of a two-dimensional convolution to the time-series vectors, which are then fed into Gated TCN to capture temporal dependencies. At each layer, the Gated TCN output is combined with dynamically learned self-adaptive graphs and passed through a GCN, enabling the model to learn evolving spatial correlations across monitoring locations. To facilitate stable training and enhance representational capacity, residual connections are incorporated to preserve gradient flow, while skip connections integrate multi-scale features across successive layers. A final two-dimensional convolution is applied to refine the aggregated spatial features. The architectural details of the Gated TCN and GCN modules are elaborated in the subsequent sections.
(1) Gated TCN.
The Gated TCN module comprises two parallel temporal convolution layers: TCN-a and TCN-b. TCN-a applies a hyperbolic tangent (tanh) activation function to extract salient temporal features from the input sequence, while TCN-b utilizes a sigmoid activation to generate a gating signal that modulates the information flow. This gating operation determines the extent to which features extracted by TCN-a are retained or suppressed. The final output is computed through an element-wise gated fusion of the two pathways, allowing the model to selectively emphasize temporally informative signals. 2.2.2. Text Transformer
Following the tokenization of the LLM, a Text Transformer extracts low-dimensional temporal features from high-dimensional textual vectors, thereby facilitating feature fusion on a unified scale. As depicted in Fig. 5, the Text Transformer architecture follows the standard Transformer structure [40], incorporating the multi-head attention mechanism while excluding the feed-forward network. This architectural adjustment is strategically introduced to reduce the risk of overfitting, particularly in the context of limited text data availability.
As shown in Fig. 5, this module comprises two primary components: a dimensionality-reducing decoder and a Transformer architecture. The decoder first compresses the high-dimensional textual embeddings generated by the LLM into a lower-dimensional latent space, facilitating efficient processing. Subsequently, the Transformer captures contextual dependencies across the input sequence. To be more specific, the lower-dimensional textual vectors are passed through the multi-head attention mechanism, where the scaled dot-product attention computes the output for each attention head. The outputs from all attention heads are then concatenated and linearly projected to produce the final output. An attention-based fusion module is employed to integrate spatial and temporal representations by selectively emphasizing the most salient components. This mechanism captures interdependencies across modalities, thereby enhancing the model's capacity to align and prioritize critical features during the fusion process.

2.3. Quantile prediction
The predictive output of the attention-based fusion module is formulated using a quantile-based approach, thereby enabling the estimation of uncertainty within the forecasting process. To optimize the GTAF model performance and facilitate rigorous evaluation, quantile loss is employed in conjunction with standard performance metrics. The selected quantiles delineate the lower and upper bounds of the predictive distribution. For instance, the 0.05 and 0.95 quantiles correspond to a 90 % confidence interval. This formulation allows the model to characterize the inherent uncertainty and variability of future outcomes, offering a probabilistic perspective essential for robust temporal prediction.
