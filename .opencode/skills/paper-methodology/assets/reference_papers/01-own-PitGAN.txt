2. Methodology
This section presents the methodology for PitGAN, a surrogate modeling approach developed to predict excavation-induced vertical soil displacement fields. As shown in Figure 1, the approach follows three main steps: (1) region-adaptive data generation, (2) data preprocessing through spatially embedded fusion, and (3) cGAN-based surrogate model development. Together, these steps establish a region-specific surrogate model that encodes geotechnical factors within their spatial context and produces a full-field soil displacement matrix for reliable prediction and risk management.
 
Figure 1 Overall framework of PitGAN surrogate modeling approach.
2.1 Framework of PitGAN surrogate modeling approach
As illustrated in Figure 1, this approach comprises three key steps, summarized as follows:
a)	Step 1 generates PitGAN data comprising input–label pairs. Key parameters representing geotechnical variations are sampled using LHS to generate foundation pit input datasets for NSM and the PitGAN model. Batch simulations in FLAC3D with a hydro-mechanical coupling method yield vertical soil displacement fields as label data.
b)	Step 2 processes PitGAN data to build a database for surrogate model training. Unstable samples are removed, and the remaining datasets—comprising excavation parameters and vertical displacement fields—are processed via spatially embedded multivariate fusion and interpolation, then structured into input–label paired matrices for supervised learning.
c)	Step 3 develops the PitGAN model using a cGAN architecture with a generator and a discriminator. The generator learns physical patterns and maps input-output relationships, while the discriminator distinguishes synthetic from real images and computes adversarial loss. The soil displacement database supports iterative adversarial training, yielding a generator capable of accurately predicting vertical soil displacement fields.
2.2 Generation of PitGAN data for modeling foundation pit excavation
This subsection describes the generation of PitGAN data, including parameter selection, sampling, and batch numerical simulation. As illustrated in Figure 2a, a simplified excavation model is defined, and 24 key parameters are identified to represent regionally variable conditions. LHS is applied to sample this multi-parameter space, generating an input dataset that serves as both raw input for PitGAN and simulation conditions for NSM. Numerical models are constructed in batches, and vertical soil displacements responses are computed using a hydro-mechanical coupling method that simulates excavation and dewatering. The resulting PitGAN dataset consists of input parameter sets and corresponding displacement matrices along the excavation profile.
2.2.1 Key parameter identification and sampling for foundation pit
In this paper, a two-dimensional plane strain model is adopted to simulate foundation pit excavation, exploiting symmetry to reduce the computational domain to half of the cross-section, as shown in Figure 2. All cases assume a bottom-up excavation method with a retaining system comprising a diaphragm wall, horizontal concrete struts, and vertical engineering piles. To enable regional adaptability, 24 tunable parameters that significantly influence excavation-induced soil deformation are identified, covering key geotechnical variations in construction conditions. These parameters are categorized into three groups—pit design parameters, soil physical properties, and construction-related variables [41–43]—with corresponding symbols listed in Table 1. Parameter ranges are defined based on geological surveys and engineering experience in typical foundation pit projects in the target region to cover a broad spectrum of realistic excavation scenarios. Notably, as excavation depths in most practical projects rarely exceed 18 m and pit widths are typically less than 120 m [31,32], the simulation domain is limited to the upper seven soil layers, excluding deeper strata to reduce model complexity and focus on the primary deformation zone [44,45]. The first backfill soil layer is merged with the second layer due to negligible thickness and similar mechanical properties.

To standardize subsequent batch calculations in NSM, the excavation model shown in Figure 2 (a) uses fixed soil domain dimensions (H0 and B0) for all simulation samples. These dimensions are determined from the defined ranges of pit geometry and soil layer thickness and aligned with the matrix format required by the PitGAN model. According to relevant studies [46,47], the maximum influence zone of excavation-induced surface settlement can extend up to four times the excavation depth, requiring sufficient coverage for modeling stability. To ensure sufficient spatial coverage and provide a conservative buffer, the soil domain width is set as B0 ≥B1 + 4.5H1. In the vertical direction, H0 is set to exceed the cumulative thickness of the top seven soil layers to fully cover the deformation zone. If this total thickness is smaller than B0, then H0 is set equal to B0, with the remaining depth filled by extending the seventh soil layer, ensuring consistent input-label matrix dimensions in both directions for standardized model construction. By default, as excavation depths H₁ rarely exceed 18 m and pit widths B₁ remain below 60 m, both H0 and B0 are fixed at 144 m. This dimension satisfies spatial coverage requirements and aligns with the PitGAN model’s input–output matrix format.
After defining parameter categories and ranges, they are combined to generate input condition datasets, which subsequently guide NSM to produce soil displacement fields for surrogate model training. Given the high dimensionality of the multi-parameter space, computing all possible combinations exhaustively is computationally prohibitive. Thus, LHS is adopted to efficiently generate a uniform, representative distribution of parameter combinations, enabling the model to learn diverse deformation patterns from a limited dataset [48]. LHS divides each parameter range into equally probable, non-overlapping intervals and samples randomly from each, generating uniformly distributed combinations across the multi-parameter space [49]. During sampling, continuous variables (e.g., soil porosity) are inverse-transformed by scaling sample values, whereas discrete variables (e.g., diaphragm wall thickness) are numerically encoded by index mapping. In addition, the LHS sample size should balance surrogate model accuracy and NSM computational efficiency, as insufficient samples hinder learning while excessive ones impose high computational costs. Based on evidence that key performance metrics stabilize at an LHS sample size of 3000 [50], and supported by prior studies [37,40], 3,000 samples are used as a balanced and effective training set size for PitGAN.
2.2.2 Numerical simulation batch calculation
Based on the generated input condition dataset, excavation models are constructed in FLAC3D, numerical analysis software based on continuum mechanics and the explicit finite difference method [51]. FLAC3D is well suited for simulating complex geotechnical problems, and its integration with the embedded IPython console enables efficient execution of large-scale batch simulations for foundation pit models. Batch calculations are executed via a Python script using the Itasca library to compute vertical soil displacement at nodes along the foundation pit profile for each set of input conditions, and the results are exported as CSV files. Following the schematic in Figure 2a, the numerical model established in FLAC3D is shown in Figure 3, with detailed construction steps provided in Appendix A. To capture the differential influence of individual soil layers on excavation-induced deformation, the model divides the soil domain into multiple layers with distinct physical properties, incorporating vertical spatial variability to capture the heterogeneous contributions of different layers. Moreover, the soil elements adopt the Hardening Soil Small Strain (HSS) model, which accounts for shear hardening, compression hardening, and shear modulus decay at small strains [43]. The well-defined parameters of the HSS model enable effective characterization of soft soil behavior, contributing to its widespread use in foundation pit simulations. In this paper, HSS model parameters are derived from empirical relationships based on the soil void ratio [52], as detailed in Table 2.
 
Figure 3 Schematic diagram of the established numerical model in NSM: (a) Schematic diagram of numerical model; (b) Front view of numerical model.
In the construction stages simulated in NSM, only the process from initial to final excavation is considered, excluding activities such as bottom slab pouring and removal of temporary supports. Three sequential stages are modeled and executed in the simulation—dewatering of the phreatic layer, soil excavation, and support installation—until the final excavation depth is reached, as detailed in Table 3. Specifically, the dewatering depth is set 1 m below the corresponding excavation depth to ensure effective water control. Excavation is maintained 0.5 m above the next support installation level to allow adequate space for support construction and installation. Soil response during these stages is simulated using a hydro-mechanical coupling method. This method integrates Darcy’s law for groundwater flow and Biot’s consolidation theory for soil deformation to capture the interaction between seepage and mechanical behavior [51]. The excavation stage is simulated by assigning a Null constitutive model to the excavated solid zones, while the dewatering process is modeled by setting fluid regions to Null. Additionally, the construction timeline is controlled by regulating the total duration of fluid flow. Each stage—dewatering, excavation, and support installation—is assigned an equal time interval T, ensuring a consistent and simplified schedule across all stages.

2.3 Preprocessing of PitGAN data for surrogate model training
This subsection outlines the preprocessing of PitGAN data for effective surrogate model training. The input originates from the foundation pit parameter dataset but is first encoded using a spatially embedded multivariate fusion method rather than being inputted directly into the model. Inspired by the RGB channel mechanism, geotechnical parameters are categorized by type and embedded into distinct 144×144 matrix channels to preserve spatial correlations and construction context. This matrix format is based on the default excavation model dimensions defined in Section 2.2, ensuring sufficient spatial coverage and supporting layer-by-layer convolution and down-sampling without introducing interpolation artifacts or dimension mismatches. Similarly, output data—representing vertical soil displacement fields along the pit profile—are formatted as 144×144 matrices to match the input structure. The spatial resolution is set to a default point spacing of 1 m in both directions and can be adjusted as needed. For visualization, these matrices are further converted into color-mapped images.
2.3.1 Generation of input matrices with spatially embedded multivariate fusion
This paper proposes a spatially embedded multivariate fusion method to generate structured 2D input matrices for the PitGAN model. The method integrates multi-source geotechnical parameters into a unified matrix while retaining spatial correlations among soil layers and retaining structures. By embedding spatial context, it provides essential cues for capturing high-dimensional dependencies across engineering components, enhancing model robustness and generalization capability across diverse excavation scenarios. Moreover, encoding multiple variables into compact matrix channels reduces computational costs from excessive input channels and prevents exponential growth in training data often seen in conventional surrogate models handling numerous parameters [53]. 
The method is based on the spatial logic of real-world foundation pit engineering, where design parameters inherently reflect spatial relationships between pit geometry and subsurface retaining structures. For example, horizontal strut spacing governs vertical distribution, while vertical pile spacing determines lateral layout in relation to pit width and depth. Similarly, soil physical properties exhibit clear vertical stratification, influencing deformation behavior layer by layer. Following this rationale, the method constructs three distinct input channels for the PitGAN model, as shown in Figure 4. The first channel is the foundation pit shape matrix, encoding excavation dimensions and support structure layout. The second channel is the soil information matrix, capturing layer depth and porosity to reflect subsurface heterogeneity. The third channel contains four scalar construction-related parameters listed in Table 1, which cannot be spatially mapped into matrices. These scalars are not embedded directly into the 2D matrices but are incorporated through a dedicated integration scheme described in Section 2.4.

The generation procedures for the first two input channels are described in Appendix B. The foundation pit shape matrix (Figure 4d) is generated using a weighted mapping scheme that integrates pit geometry parameters (H1 and B1), retaining structure design (Hc, Hs, R, and Bp), and bottom reinforcement (Hr and Br) into a unified matrix. This matrix encodes the spatial configuration of subsurface structures, forming a cross-sectional representation of the retaining system. Structural components are assigned distinct weights based on type, with overlapping areas—such as junctions between diaphragm walls and vertical piles—further emphasized through cumulative weighting to reflect higher structural importance. The soil information matrix (Figure 4e) is constructed by embedding soil layer porosity and thickness, along with bottom reinforcement information. This matrix preserves soil vertical heterogeneity, producing a spatially structured matrix that resembles a geological profile. Porosity values are assigned to the depth range of each layer, while the reinforced pit bottom is encoded with a fixed value of 0.6 to represent its compacted characteristics and increased stiffness. For both channels, the default resolution is 1 m in both horizontal and vertical directions. Additionally, to capture non-integer boundaries (e.g., structural depths, layer interfaces), linear interpolation is applied, as shown in Figure 4b. Together, these spatially structured inputs enable PitGAN to learn spatial configurations and material variability inherent in deep excavations.
2.3.2 Generation of label matrices
The label data for supervised PitGAN training represent vertical soil displacement fields along the foundation pit profile, generated via batch numerical simulations as described in Section 2.2.2. During batch simulations, convergence is deemed achieved when the model’s unbalanced force remains below 2×10-6 or the fluid calculation time exceeds 2T. These criteria help prevent model non-convergence, pit instability, and excessive displacement caused by unsuitable input conditions. However, some simulations still fail to meet engineering stability requirements. To further refine the dataset, the Factor of Safety (FS) against bottom uplift is applied to re-filter displacement results, retaining only samples that meet the FS threshold. FS is estimated from an empirical correlation with the maximum bottom heave (δm) [41], as given by Eq. (1). Subsequently, each filtered vertical displacement CSV file is converted into a high-precision displacement matrix aligned to the 144×144 grid required for PitGAN output, as illustrated in Figure 5. Due to the non-uniform mesh and irregular node spacing in the FLAC3D simulations, the raw displacement data do not conform to this uniform grid. To address this, height interpolation is applied to generate an aligned displacement matrix, with the formula shown in Figure 5c. Additionally, to improve convergence and training efficiency, all input and label matrices are normalized via Min-Max scaling to [0, 1]. After filtering unstable samples and normalizing, a comprehensive dataset is built, with processed three-channel input paired with corresponding displacement matrices. Normalization parameters are retained to allow denormalization of model outputs, ensuring accurate recovery of physical displacement.

2.4 Establishment of PitGAN model for predicting vertical soil displacement field
This subsection describes the architecture of the PitGAN model, which is specifically designed to predict vertical soil displacement fields during deep excavation. PitGAN is built upon the architecture of cGAN [56], comprising a generator and a discriminator to learn through adversarial competition. The generator produces synthetic vertical soil displacement matrices that closely match real displacement fields, while the discriminator learns to distinguish between synthetic outputs and ground-truth data. Through adversarial training under supervised learning, PitGAN progressively improves predictive accuracy, ultimately producing a robust generator capable of delivering realistic, high-resolution predictions of excavation-induced vertical soil displacement.
2.4.1 Architecture of generator
The generator employs a U-Net architecture [57], comprising an encoder (down-sampling) and a decoder (up-sampling), as illustrated in Figure 6. It accepts three input channels: (1) the foundation pit shape matrix and (2) the soil information matrix, as described in Section 2.3.1, and (3) four scalar construction-related parameters listed in Table 1. Since these scalars cannot be directly encoded as spatially structured matrices, they are processed through a dedicated multiset integration module before entering the encoder (see top-left of Figure 6). This module projects the scalar vector into a higher-dimensional latent space via two fully connected layers (FC1 and FC2), then reshapes it into a 144×144 matrix to match the dimensions of the other channels. This embedding ensures compatibility with convolutional operations while enabling the network to capture latent interactions between global construction-related features and localized soil-structure patterns. The resulting scalar matrix is concatenated with the other two input matrices to form the complete three-channel input to the encoder.
Once the input is prepared, the encoder’s convolutional layers extract both spatial and parametric features from three channels, progressively capturing higher-dimensional characteristics, including vertical spatial variability in soil physical properties and interactions between soil layers and retaining structures. Conversely, the decoder’s deconvolutional layers gradually restore the feature maps to their original spatial resolution, reproducing the physical laws of soil-water-structure coupling mechanism and generating the soil displacement matrix. Moreover, skip connections are used to pass feature maps directly from the encoder to the corresponding decoder layers, preserving high-resolution details and integrating multi-scale information. The architecture and output tensor dimensions for each layer are shown in Figure 6. Each encoding block includes a convolutional layer, batch normalization, and a Leaky ReLU activation function. Each decoding block includes a deconvolutional layer, batch normalization, and a ReLU activation function, while the final decoding layer employs a deconvolutional operation followed by a hyperbolic tangent activation. Additionally, to reduce noise and address uncertainties in soil constitutive parameters, a dropout rate of 0.5 is applied after the first three decoding layers to prevent overfitting and improve training stability.
 
Figure 6 Schematic diagram of the generator architecture.
Notably, excavation-induced vertical soil displacement typically exhibits two critical peak patterns: ground settlement outside the pit—where the influence zone spans roughly four times the excavation depth—and bottom uplift near the diaphragm wall [58]. Although occupying a small portion in the displacement matrix, these regions are deformation-intensive and strongly influenced by structural and geotechnical features, posing a key challenge for the model to emphasize them. To address this, the Attention Gate (AG) [59] is integrated into the U-Net architecture, functioning alongside skip connections, as illustrated at the top of Figure 6. The AG takes two inputs: a shallow feature map x from the encoder and a gating signal g from the decoder that restores the coarse displacement pattern. Through linear transformations and element-wise summation, the AG computes attention coefficients via sigmoid activation, which are then used to reweight x. This operation selectively amplifies displacement-relevant features—particularly those in peak deformation zones—while suppressing irrelevant features. By refining feature transmission during up-sampling process, the AG enhances the model’s focus on high-impact regions and improves overall prediction fidelity.
2.4.2 Architecture of discriminator
The discriminator adopts a Markovian PatchGAN architecture [60], as shown in Figure 7. Unlike traditional GAN discriminators that output a single scalar for global authenticity, it produces a spatial judgment matrix in which each entry indicates the probability that the corresponding region in the generated matrix matches the label matrix. This architecture is well suited for capturing localized deformation patterns—such as bottom heave and ground settlement—which typically occur in small, high-gradient regions of the displacement matrix. The discriminator is a fully convolutional network for down-sampling, with each module comprising a convolutional layer, batch normalization, and a ReLU activation. Its inputs include the foundation pit condition matrix, the generated matrix from the generator, and the label matrix. After concatenation and down-sampling, the discriminator outputs the judgment matrix. To better capture complex geotechnical interactions, it incorporates matrices encoding underground structures and soil layers, improving its ability to model spatially coupled deformation mechanisms. Moreover, to determine the optimal resolution for the judgment matrix, the kernel size and stride of the final convolutional layer are adjusted to generate judgment matrices of varying sizes. Comparative experiments (Appendix C) indicate that an 8×8 configuration delivers the best performance. This resolution enables localized assessment, enhancing the generator’s ability to focus on accurately modeling critical peak deformation zones that are otherwise difficult to predict.

2.4.3 Definition of function loss and evaluation metric
During adversarial training, the generator gradually produces synthetic data x′ that approximates the real label data x by learning underlying physical information through extracted high-dimensional features. However, when the generator is updated using the sigmoid cross-entropy loss, a vanishing-gradient problem may arise, particularly when samples fall on the correct side of the decision boundary yet still deviate substantially from the real data [61]. To mitigate this issue, PitGAN adopts the least-squares loss from Least Squares Generative Adversarial Networks (LSGAN) [61], which improves training stability and alleviates gradient vanishing. Additionally, an L1 loss term is added to the generator loss to promote smoother displacement fields and suppress abrupt changes or noise during training.
