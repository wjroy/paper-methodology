2. Method

2.1. Framework of the proposed TSFRA

2.1.1. Problem definition

This study aims to conduct a risk assessment focusing on evaluating the impact of excavation activities on adjacent buildings across various stages and locations. The assessment relies on the information fusion from expert judgment and historical settlement data. To accomplish this objective, a monitoring system equipped with numerous sensors is deployed along the perimeter of the adjacent buildings to continuously monitor and record real-time cumulative settlement.

Each sensor is strategically located and designated as a monitoring point (identified by labels such as JFxx, for example, JF42-JF85). While the building is influenced by the long-term effects of excavation activities at a nearby foundation pit, the construction process is divided into different construction conditions (denoted as ax, e.g., A1-A7), following the chronological order of the excavation stages.

2.1.2. Overview

To comprehensively assess the impact of excavation-induced risk on the surrounding buildings, the TSFRA model incorporating FAHP, DGDT, and CM is developed. The architecture of TSFRA is shown in Fig. 1, which is made up of four key phases to form a systematic decision support tool.

(1) Multi-sensor information collection: Sensors are strategically positioned around the targeted building to automatically and periodically record ground settlement at various monitoring points, reflecting the risk status influenced by excavation. Then, these data can be reasonably cropped based on the progression sequence of excavation conditions for deeper investigations.

(2) Temporal weight determination with FAHP: In the temporal dimension, excavation work exerts dynamic effects on the risk status of surrounding structures over time. With the assistance of FAHP, several experts in underground engineering are invited to apply triangular fuzzy numbers to quantify fuzzy weights associated with structures at key stages.

Step 2: DEMATEL method implementation

DEMATEL primarily focuses on the strength of causal relationships within the system, aiming to provide quantitative results of causal connections in a matrix form. It can quantify the influence intensity among various factors using centrality indicators, resulting in the identification of crucial nodes that play a decisive role in a complex excavation system.

DEMATEL operates directly on outputs A = (aij)n×n from DAG-GNN. It replaces expert ratings for pairwise factor assessments with a weighted adjacency matrix, which can quantify binary relationships between elements. To begin with, an impact matrix is constructed to measure the direct influence of one monitoring point on another, which is then normalized in Eq. (10).

After that, a comprehensive impact matrix T of the system is calculated according to Eq. (11). Lastly, the centrality Ci of variable Xi is calculated by adding the influence degree Di and the degree of being influenced Ri using Eq. (12). It measures the importance degree of monitoring points within the excavation system.

N = (nij)n×n = (aij/max(∑j=1n aij))n×n

(Eq. 10)

where max(∑j=1n aij) represents the maximum value of all elements in the i-th row of the matrix.

T = (tij)n×n = N(I - N)^(-1)

(Eq. 11)

where N is the normalized impact matrix, I ∈ R^(n×n) is the identity matrix, and (I - N)^(-1) is the inverse matrix of (I - N).

Ci = Di + Ri = ∑j=1n tij + ∑j=1n tji, i = 1, 2, ..., n

(Eq. 12)

In short, the core idea behind DEMATEL is to rank the factors based on the mutual influences among them. Although DEMATEL can effectively capture internal causal relationships within the excavation system, it still owns some limitations to be noted.

Firstly, the complexity of matrix transformations makes it difficult to gain an understanding of the foundation pit system, which will unfortunately weaken the operability of computation and analysis. Secondly, DEMATEL primarily focuses on internal causal relationships within the system, making it relatively challenging to capture the overall dynamic nature of the system over time. Thus, there is a need to introduce other efficient and intuitive methods to handle the system's complexity.

Step 3: Topological analysis

To address DEMATEL's limitations, topological analysis is carried out to offer decision-makers an intuitive visualization of the excavation system's structure. Relying on the graphic, nodes in the structure represent monitoring points with time-series data, and connections signify causal relationships revealed by DAG-GNN.

This analysis uncovers intricate relationships within the system, shedding light on the dynamic nature of excavation-related risks. For dynamic systems, topological analysis at the microscopic level can also help to investigate the temporal evolution process of networks by tracking the changes in nodes and connections over time.

Herein, metrics such as clustering coefficient and PageRank in Eqs. (13) and (14) are utilized for micro-level analysis, indicating densely connected clusters and node influence, respectively. Higher values of these metrics may indicate localized structural instability or potential vulnerabilities in the excavation system.
