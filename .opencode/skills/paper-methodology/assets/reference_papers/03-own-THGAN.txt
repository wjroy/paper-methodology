3.	Methodology
3.1 Overall framework of digital twin
As shown in Fig. 3, this study develops a digital twin framework by integrating BIM, IBS-based excavation models, and THGAN. The framework bridges the physical excavation system and its digital counterpart, enabling reliable prediction of the spatiotemporal evolution of multi-source risks. Specifically, the framework consists of three core modules.
 
Fig. 3. The overall framework of digital twin.
(a) Data–Mechanism–Hybrid (DMH) feature generation: This module derives mechanics-informed DMH features from BIM and field monitoring data to preserve physical fidelity in the digital twin. It constructs an Incremental Beam–Spring (IBS) excavation model to simulate wall–soil interaction and stiffness disturbances, and inverts DMH feature by integrating the IBS model with PSO algorithm to reconstruct soil and strut mechanical states across construction stages.
(b) Multi-Risk Heterogeneous (MRH) graph generation: This module integrates multi-source data from BIM, monitoring data, and DMH features into MRH graphs, providing a graph-based digital representation of multi-risk states. Each MRH graph includes a node-feature matrix that embeds multi-dimensional attributes and adjacency matrices constructed under a dual spatiotemporal dependency scheme to encode heterogeneous relations and temporal dependencies.
(c) Temporal Heterogeneous Graph Attention Network (THGAN): This module develops THGAN to learn spatiotemporal patterns on MRH graphs and forecast deformation-related risks across excavation stages. It comprises heterogeneous graph attention networks (HGAN) that capture relation-aware spatial dependencies through node-level attention and pattern-level attention across relations, and gated recurrent units with temporal attention (GRU-TA) for time-aware temporal modeling.
During practical deployment, THGAN is trained offline using historical data and deployed with fixed weights. Each BIM-synchronized update initiates PSO-based DMH feature inversion and MRH graph updating, followed by THGAN inference for multi-risk forecasting and timely decision-making.
3.2 Data-mechanism-hybrid feature generation module
The mechanical response of deep excavations is governed by processes such as unloading and dewatering, which induce complex deformation patterns. Extracting implicit mechanical-state features from BIM-synchronized project data is therefore essential to improve the physical interpretability and responsiveness of the digital twin. This section introduces the DMH feature generation module, which links the digital twin to the underlying physical behavior of the excavation system.
3.2.1 Mechanical modeling of deep excavation
To extract excavation-induced mechanical responses from BIM-synchronized data, a robust mechanical model is required. The two-dimensional Beam-Spring (BS) model is widely used to predict retaining-wall deformation along a pit profile due to its accuracy and practical applicability (Nikkhah et al., 2017). In this model, the retaining wall is idealized as vertically oriented beam elements. Soil forces on the non-excavated side are represented by earth pressure, while the excavation-side soil and struts are modeled as an equivalent spring system (Cui et al., 2024). The BS parameters therefore provide interpretable descriptors of soil and support mechanical states, which form the physical basis of the DMH feature generation module. Traditional total-load beam–spring (TBS) formulations compute wall deformation from the accumulated loading history. However, they do not explicitly capture stage-wise changes in soil springs and earth pressure, which limits their ability to represent the nonlinear evolution of soil resistance. To address this issue, this study adopts an incremental beam–spring (IBS) model, as shown in Fig. 4(a). The IBS model follows the same load–resistance equivalence principle as TBS, but introduces incremental loads between successive construction stages to capture the nonlinear evolution of the load–resistance response.
To capture nonlinear load variations, the incremental loads include the excavation-side soil resistance change (d [Fp]), the non-excavation-side earth pressure change (d [Fe]), and the servo strut axial-force change (d [Faxial]). The deformation response is computed using the matrix displacement method. The global stiffness matrices and incremental load vector are assembled to form the equilibrium equation in Equation (1), and the wall deflection increment is obtained from Equation (2).

3.2.2 DMH feature inversion and update based on mechanical simulation
Based on the IBS-based mechanics model, this study introduces a set of parameters to characterize the mechanical state of the soil–support system. These parameters are not directly available from BIM records and exhibit stage-wise variability due to excavation unloading and support stiffness changes. To identify these latent parameters from measured responses, an PSO-based DMH feature inversion procedure is formulated by integrating BIM data, the IBS model, and PSO algorithm (Kennedy and Eberhart, 1995). PSO is adopted as a derivative-free search strategy to identify a parameter set that best reconciles the IBS-simulated incremental deformation with the measured incremental deformation. 
As illustrated in Fig. 5, the procedure consists of data preparation, model initialization, and PSO-based parameter identification. During data preparation, excavation and monitoring records are collected, and the measured wall deflection series is denoised using a five-point cubic smoothing method (X. Wang et al., 2023) to reduce errors associated with inclinometer noise and construction-induced disturbance. A five-point interval sampling scheme is then applied to compute wall deflection increments, which aligns the monitoring input with the incremental formulation adopted in the IBS model and further suppresses measurement fluctuations. During model initialization, the IBS model is instantiated using site-specific excavation information. Feasible ranges are specified for the inversion parameters ([m], [ks], [kr] and [ka]), and the PSO population is initialized within these bounds. For each monitoring sample, PSO evaluates candidate parameter sets by running the IBS model and computing the mismatch between the simulated incremental deformation d [∆] and the measured incremental deformation d [s] using the objective function in Equation (16). Particle velocities and positions are updated according to Equations (17) and (18) based on the particle-best solution Pbest and the global-best solution Gbest. The inversion terminates when the maximum iteration number Ni is reached or when the objective value satisfies a prescribed convergence criterion, yielding the identified parameter set (m, ks, kr and ka). These identified parameters are then organized as the DMH features and used as input attributes in the subsequent MRH graph construction.

During deployment in real projects, the DMH feature generation module is configured with project-specific soil properties (e.g., soft clay, stiff clay, or sand) and support-system details from on-site investigations. The IBS formulation and inversion workflow remain unchanged across projects. PSO parameter bounds are initialized from local codes and project reports and then refined during inversion using monitoring records, enabling the inferred mechanical-state features to adapt to different soil behaviors. 3.3 Multi-risk heterogeneous graph generation module
DMH features and BIM data provide a high-quality data foundation. To represent multi-source risks and their interactions, multi-source information must be integrated into a unified digital form. This section presents the MRH graph generation module, which constructs MRH graphs, node feature matrices, and adjacency matrices.

Field monitoring sensors such as retaining wall deflection points and ground settlement points are represented as nodes. 3.3.2 Construction of adjacent matrices in different relation patterns
To quantify associations among risk nodes, adjacency matrices are constructed for each relation type. Instead of relying on a single data-driven adjacency, a multi-adjacency strategy is adopted under a dual spatiotemporal mechanism, as described below. Notably, all adjacency matrices are binary connectivity matrices, and self-loops are added to adjacencies by setting diagonal entries to 1.
(a) Construction of spatial adjacency matrices: Spatial adjacencies represent geometric proximity and spatial interactions among risk points within MRH graphs. For the VA→VA and VB→VB relation types, the adjacency matrices describe interactions among homogeneous nodes. These interactions reflect the continuity of retaining-wall deformation and ground response, where localized disturbances can propagate to nearby locations. A radius-based method (Kovalenko et al., 2022) combined with the Pearson correlation coefficient (Sedgwick, 2012) is used to determine the influence range and construct the adjacency matrix, as summarized in Algorithm 1. The Pearson correlation coefficient Pr is first computed for each node pair from the recorded deformation series. Node pairs with significant correlation (|Pr|≥rp) are retained, and the maximum spatial distance among the retained pairs is taken as the influence radius R. The spatial adjacency matrix is then constructed based on R.  For the VA→VB relation type, a separate spatial adjacency matrix is used to describe heterogeneous coupling. Ground-settlement analyses indicate that retaining-wall deflection can induce vertical displacement in adjacent soil layers, and differential deformation along wall depth contributes to surface settlement (Lu-yao et al., 2016). Therefore, all cross-type entries between VA and VB nodes are set to 1, indicating that each retaining-wall point is allowed to influence the ground-settlement points.

Step 2: apply the correlation threshold |Pr[i,j]| ≥rp to filter significantly correlated node pairs.
Step 3: determine the influence radius R1 and R2 based on correlated node pairs within VA and VB. 
Step 4: construct adjacent matrices A1 and A2 for VA→VA and VB→VB relation types, respectively:

       end for

(b) Construction of temporal adjacency matrix: The temporal adjacency matrix is derived from the Granger Causality (GC) test (Granger, 2001) to encode temporal dependencies as node connections in the MRH graph. The GC test is widely used in multivariate time-series analysis and assesses whether the past values of one variable improve the prediction of another, thereby indicating causal relationships in a statistical sense (Stokes and Purdon, 2017). This provides a statistical basis for describing temporal influence patterns among risk nodes, and the construction process is presented in Algorithm 2. First, the deformation series at each node is tested for stationarity using the Augmented Dickey–Fuller (ADF) test (Mushtaq, 2011). If the series is non-stationary, first-order differencing is applied and the ADF test is repeated until stationarity is achieved. Next, a lag order L is specified and regression models are fitted for each node pair (xi, xj). The baseline model predicts xj using only its own lagged values (Equation 20), whereas the extended model additionally includes the lagged terms of xi in (Equation 21). Subsequently, the causality matrix AGC is constructed from the GC test results, where AGC [i, j] denotes the p-value for testing whether xi Granger-causes xj. The temporal adjacency matrix A4 is obtained by thresholding AGC at rGC. Although the ADF test and differencing are applied to satisfy the stationarity prerequisite, the GC test still assumes linear autoregressive dependence, which can be challenged by nonlinear deformation in deep excavations. Accordingly, A4 is treated as a soft statistical prior of temporal dependence rather than a mechanistic causal graph. Its contribution is regulated by relation-pattern attention in THGAN, which can down-weight unreliable temporal messages and shifting weight to other relation patterns when the temporal prior is imperfect.

3.4 THGAN implementation for spatiotemporal evolution modeling
To capture multi-factor coupling and cross-pattern spatiotemporal dependencies in MRH graphs, this study proposes THGAN to extract hybrid spatiotemporal features and build the digital twin model for deep excavation. The core components of THGAN are described below.
3.4.1 Architecture of THGAN
The overall architecture of THGAN is shown in Fig. 7. It consists of HGAN and GRU-TA modules, incorporating attention at the node, relation-pattern, and temporal levels. THGAN takes a historical sequence of MRH graphs as input, learns the spatiotemporal evolution mapping F, and uses Equation (22) to predict the deformation at each risk point for the next T days. The HGAN module first processes the node feature matrix X and the adjacency matrices {Ar} from the input MRH graphs to capture cross-pattern spatial dependencies. Within HGAN, the node-level attention layer computes the weights between each node and its neighbors and aggregates neighbor information to obtain relation-specific node embeddings  . Relation-pattern attention then reweights different relation patterns and integrates them into a cross-pattern node embedding Ht. These embeddings are fed into the GRU-TA module to model temporal evolution. The GRU captures sequential dependencies, and temporal attention refines the hidden states to emphasize informative time steps and extract key time-varying patterns. Finally, the resulting spatiotemporal node representations are passed through a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) to produce the deformation prediction graph.

3.4.3 GRU-TA module for modeling cross-temporal hybrid dependency
The cross-pattern fused node embeddings { } from HGAN modules are then fed into the GRU-TA module to model cross-temporal hybrid dependencies. This module includes a GRU layer, a temporal attention layer, and an MLP layer. The GRU captures sequential dependencies in the embedding sequence. Temporal attention reweights time steps to emphasize informative history and refine time-aware embeddings. The MLP maps the final embedding to the prediction space to generate future deformation. Details of each layer are described below.
(a) GRU layer: A GRU is used to model temporal dependencies and non-stationary evolution in retaining wall deformation and ground settlement (Zhao et al., 2020). The update and reset gates balance short- and long-term information and adapt to stage-dependent deformation patterns. After foundation slab casting, deformation tends to stabilize and longer-term dependencies become more relevant. Compared with LSTM, the GRU removes the independent forget gate, which reduces parameter size and helps mitigate overfitting while retaining effective temporal modeling capability (Zhao et al., 2020).
