3. Methods
The overarching framework of the proposed methodology is illustrated in Fig. 2, with detailed components shown in Fig. 3. The process begins with the application of a TSFM to forecast the historical multivariate dataset, thereby generating probabilistic predictions for the meteorological factors and all irradiance variables across each channel. Notably, these multiple probabilistic predictions are feature-encoded across three distinct channels. Secondly, to leverage the generalization capability of the TSFM and enhance the precision of the forecasts, a dual-channel spatio-temporal feature encoder is deployed. This encoder is designed to predict future irradiance across multiple sites, utilizing both historical data and the aforementioned probabilistic forecasts.
In the realm of future temporal feature extraction, this paper strategically implements a Tensor Regression Network (TRN) to replace traditional fully connected layers for performing regression analysis on high-dimensional feature spaces. Unlike fully connected layers, which are prone to disrupting the inherent structure within high-dimensional features, the proposed TR method adeptly retains the structural interrelations among variables.
3.1. Channel-independent prediction based on TSFMs
Initially, the TSFMs are employed to generate a preliminary forecast for each variable within the historical dataset. Fig. 4 shows the tokenization and inference process of TSFMs. Chronos [43] is a pre-trained time series forecasting model that is underpinned by a language model architecture. The time series data are converted into a sequence of tokens through a process of scaling and quantization, with the language model being trained on these tokens using a cross-entropy loss function. Post-training, the model is capable of producing probabilistic forecasts by sampling multiple future trajectories from a given historical context. Chronos has been extensively trained on a wide array of publicly available time series data, as well as synthetic data generated through Gaussian processes. The input time series is processed to create a sequence of tokens, which are then input into a language model, which could be either an encoder-decoder or a decoder-only model, and trained using a cross-entropy loss. During the inference stage, tokens are sampled autoregressively from the model and translated back into numerical values, with multiple trajectories being sampled to form a predictive distribution. In addition to training on a broad collection of public datasets, Chronos incorporates advanced data augmentation strategies, namely TSMixup and KernelSynth. These methodologies are designed to overcome the limitations associated with small training datasets in time series forecasting, thereby bolstering the model’s robustness and generalization capabilities. Chronos provides a range of pre-trained model parameters, scaling from a compact model with 5M parameters to a more extensive model, designated as Large, with a substantial 710M parameters.

Therefore, the future channel can consider a wider range of predictive possibilities, offering a certain level of robustness against sudden weather changes on one hand, and fully leveraging the capabilities of the front-end TSFM on the other.
Despite the richness of this dataset, a judicious selection of key probabilistic indicators can succinctly encapsulate the forecast’s probabilistic bounds. These indicators may include, but are not limited to, the maximum and minimum values, the mean, quantiles, and other pivotal statistical measures. To this end, within the probabilistic values channel, a linear layer network is deployed to synthesize these probabilities into a condensed feature vector of dimension 3. This feature adaptively distills and represents the critical information embedded within the probability distribution. By concentrating on these fundamental probabilistic traits, this methodology adeptly reduces information redundancy, ensuring that the model remains focused on the most salient aspects of the predictive distribution.
3.2. Dual-channel spatio-temporal encoder
The dual-channel spatio-temporal decoder is employed to integrate the forecast information from the TSFMs with historical meteorological and irradiance data. This system primarily comprises the backbone network, ConvLSTM, and the tensor regression network, which are detailed in the following sections. The backbone network, ConvLSTM, is designed to handle the spatio-temporal dynamics of the data. It leverages the strengths of convolutional layers to extract spatial features and the power of LSTM units to capture temporal dependencies. Following the ConvLSTM, the tensor regression network is introduced to further refine the feature representation and enhance the predictive accuracy. This network employs tensor decomposition techniques to capture the complex interactions within the data, allowing for a more nuanced understanding of the spatio-temporal relationships that influence solar irradiance patterns.

For the historical data, the backbone network for feature extraction is the ConvLSTM [44], which is specifically designed to address spatio-temporal data. This methodology empowers the model to capitalize on the rich tapestry of probabilistic forecasts, thereby amplifying the portrayal of the forecast’s inherent uncertainty and variability.
