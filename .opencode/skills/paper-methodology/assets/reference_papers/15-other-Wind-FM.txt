2. Methodology
2.1. Time series prompt for exogenous variables
2.1.1. Intuition
Exogenous prompts are designed to introduce exogenous information into the foundation LTSMs and guide their output. In this form, weather prediction can be incorporated into the LTSMs to enhance the accuracy of wind power predictions. In LLMs, the construction of prompts comes naturally as users can build them based on their linguistic habits. However, in the field of time series, it is not feasible for humans to directly construct prompts that align with the time series of the target variable based on an understanding of temporal patterns. Therefore, a prompt embedding is utilized to map auxiliary exogenous time series directly into the embedding vector space of the target time series, thereby constructing the prompt. 2.1.2. Method
The prompt embedding consists of several steps. Initially, the exogenous time series are embedded and projected into a high-dimensional vector space. Subsequently, normalization bias (NB) is introduced concerning the reversible instance normalization (RevIN) [36] strategy of foundation LTSMs. Foundation LTSMs are typically pre-trained on time series data from multiple sources with varying distributions, so the models often adopt RevIN to make each input time series sample have a mean value of 0 and a standard deviation of 1 along the temporal axis and denormalize the output during every forward calculation. While this approach unifies the distribution of multi-source data, it can distort domain-specific patterns and cross-variable correlations when adapting to downstream tasks. When the target variable is in different value ranges, its relationship with exogenous variables differs, while RevIN disrupts the network’s ability to perceive the value range of the target variable. Therefore, informing the model with normalization information during prompt construction is crucial. After adding normalization bias to the exogenous embedding, a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) is used to align the patterns of the target and exogenous variables and finally output prompt. 2.2. Prompt tuning for time series
2.2.1. Intuition
In LLMs, guiding model generation through prompts is a powerful intuition, leading to the development of prompt tuning. Since LTSMs and LLMs are fundamentally similar in that they both predict subsequent tokens based on an understanding of preceding content, prompt tuning is expected to be effective in foundation LTSMs. Drawing on the concept of soft prompts from LLMs, continuous prompt tokens can be constructed in the vector space of LTSMs’ embedding. These prompts can be optimized to achieve optimal guidance for LTSMs on specific downstream tasks. This fine-tuning approach requires adjusting only the soft prompts while keeping the model backbone frozen. During inference, the optimized soft prompt can be added as a prefix to enable the LTSM to adapt to new tasks. Therefore, this is a PEFT method that has higher storage and computation efficiency compared to FFT. The overall approach of PT is illustrated in Fig. 2.

2.3. Implementation
Timer [23], a pre-trained decoder-only foundation LTSM, was utilized to evaluate the performance of proposed methods. This model contains 67.40 million parameters and has been pre-trained on datasets from multiple domains, which comprise over 1 billion data points. Timer standardizes data from diverse sources using a single-series sequence (S3) format, which is a variant of Channel-independent (CI) [40], modeling each variable independently while ignoring cross-variable relationships. Timer employs a tokenization approach that splits time series into patches and treats patches as tokens.
The SDWPF dataset [41] is utilized to validate the feasibility of prompts in the context of wind power prediction. This dataset includes two years of SCADA data with a sampling frequency of 30 minutes. For experimentation, the first five wind turbines were extracted from this dataset. The 2022 version of this dataset was also used in the pretraining of Timer; however, this paper employs the 2024 version of the dataset, which has different sampling frequency. The SDWPF dataset includes meteorological data from the area surrounding the wind farm and wind speed measurements taken by the turbines themselves. Wind power predictions typically use regional wind speed forecasts to assist in power predictions. However, to eliminate the prediction error of wind speeds and the conversion error from regional wind speeds to turbine-local wind speeds, the experiment adopts turbine-measured wind speeds instead of wind speed predictions as the exogenous variable by default. Although this approach may overstate the metrics, the effectiveness of the proposed method can still be validated by comparing the relative performance of the metrics across different methods since all methods utilize the same exogenous variable. As for the meteorological wind speed, it was employed as an inaccurate exogenous variable containing noise to aid power prediction in some experiments, in order to verify the robustness of the methods.
In the experiment, the data of each wind turbine is split into 70 %, 10 %, and 20 % portions, from which the training, validation, and test samples are generated respectively. Timer and the prompt embedding use a patch size of 96, a historical power sequence length of 288 (equivalent to 3 tokens), and a prediction length of 96. The length of the wind speed sequence used for prompt matches that of the power sequence, but it lags by 96 time steps. The PT adopted a prefix length of 10. The reparameterization, normalization bias for prefix and prompt embedding utilized a single-hidden-layer MLP with a hidden size of 256. This configuration resulted in 8.96 million trainable parameters in PT which account for 13.3 % of Timer’s parameter count, and 0.63 million parameters for exogenous prompt embedding.
