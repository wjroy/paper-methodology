3. Method
This paper proposes a new approach called AC-GGN to realize a highaccuracy and real-time prediction of the excavation-induced ground
settlement at each monitoring point within the monitoring network. It is
expected to generally apply in the risk monitoring task during deep
excavation, where the risk development trend along with the non-linear
relationships among influential features is hard to accurately capture
using the traditional geotechnical model or physical knowledge. As
depicted in Fig. 1, there are three key stages in the overall flowchart of
the developed AC-GGN, including input data preparation, AC-GGN
development, model evaluation and analysis. To be more specific, the
first step begins with t-Distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (tSNE) to reduce feature complexity and preserve as much of the vari­
ability in the original data as possible. The processed data is then
explored by the GC test to explore the causal associations among the
multivariate time-series features, resulting in the creation of an adja­
cency matrix that is fed into the deep learning model. The second step
entails the development of a hybrid graphic-based model namely ACGGN with the powerful capability of capturing causal connection, and
spatial and temporal dependencies embedded in the continuous collec­
tion of monitoring data, aiming to accurately forecast the ground set­
tlement at each monitoring point several steps ahead. In the end, the
third step involves assessing and comprehending the performance of the
intelligent model. A detailed description of each stage is provided below.
3.1. Input data preparation
3.1.1. t-Distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE) for data
dimensionality reduction
The high dimensionality and complexity of the monitoring data
collected from the foundation pit site may cause a high computational
cost for learning purposes. As a solution for simplifying the modeling of
the complex problem, data dimensionality reduction can be considered
as an essential part of input data preprocessing. The core concept un­
derlying this technique is to decrease the number of random variables
while retaining pertinent information that is necessary for learning ac­
curate and predictive models.
To accomplish this goal, a statistical method named t-SNE is
deployed, as it is particularly well-suited to embed high-dimensional
data in a low-dimensional space for visualization (Van der Maaten and
Hinton, 2008). Compared to linear methods like Principal Component
Analysis (PCA) and Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA), t-SNE excels in
capturing intricate nonlinear data relationships, making its lowdimensional representations ideal for neural network integration. It
shows promising advantages of preserving local data point similarity
while maintaining computational efficiency over other methods. More
specifically, t-SNE extracts patterns based on the similarity of data
points with features and creates a probability distribution of points in a
lower-dimension space. To measure the similarity, the heavy-tailed
Student-t distribution with one degree of freedom is employed instead
of the Gaussian distribution. It allows for keeping the low-dimensional
representation of very similar data points as close as possible.
Given data xi, i = 1, …, m in a high-dimensional space and the cor­
responding N data yi, i = 1, …, m in the mapped low-dimensional space,
Eq. (1) (Hinton and Roweis, 2002) converts the high-dimensional
Euclidean distances between data points into the joint probability pj|i.
The higher pj|i always indicates a closer distance between data points.
After that, since data points in the high-dimensional space cannot leave
enough space for mapping in the low-dimensional space causing the
crowding problem, the heavy-tailed Student-t distribution should
replace the Gaussian distribution to convert the distance between xi and
xj into probability pj|i in the low-dimensional counterpart using Eq. (2).
Lastly, under the goal of t-SNE to get a low-dimensional distribution for

ment between the two distributions using Mutual entropy (also known
as Kullback-Leibler divergences (KLD)). Gradient descent on C can then
be used to learn the appropriate yi.

3.1.2. Granger causality test (GC test) for causality discovery
In the context of establishing a graph-based neural network, a crucial
aspect involves the generation of an input adjacency matrix, which de­
termines the underlying semantic representation of nodes and edges.
The objective of this task is to identify a meaningful mapping between
the real-world system and the network structure. Herein, the Granger
causality (GC) test, acting as a novel topology mapping method, is
adopted for causality discovery in multivariate time series analysis. The
GC test enables the inference of causal relationships among variables
from observational datasets, leading to the generation of an adjacency
matrix that represents the causal relations among the variables in a
graphical format.
Informally, the GC test is a statistical hypothesis test to check
whether two variables in time series form are causally related (Granger,
2001). There are two premises in the GC test: one is that a cause can
occur before its effect, and the other is that knowledge of the cause can
enhance the prediction of its effect (Stokes and Purdon, 2017). That is to
say, it can provide insights into the information flow between time se­
ries, aiming to verify the usefulness of one variable in predicting another
variable with a specific lag. In particular, the GC test only offers infor­
mation concerning predictive capability, which is not the causal rela­
tionship in the true philosophical sense. Since the GC test focuses more
on prediction performance rather than the theoretical model underlying
the prediction task, it can be reasonably carried out to produce the input
adjacency matrix reflecting the cause-and-effect relationships for the
development of the AC-GGN prediction model.

3.2. AC-GGN model development
3.2.1. AC-GGN architecture
The general architecture of the hybrid deep learning model AC-GGN
is provided in Fig. 2, which combines the capabilities of GCN, GRU, and
attention mechanism to realize either short-term or long-term predic­
tion. To be more specific, GCN and GRU satisfy the need for the inclusion
of the spatial and temporal characteristics of input data, respectively.
The input data along with the causality-based adjacency matrix are first
processed by GCN to capture time-varying spatial characteristics and
then passed to GRU for time-series forecasting. This enables the hybrid
model to effectively discover causality, perform spatial–temporal
learning, and learn graph representations. After passing the GCN and
GRU layers, the attention mechanism is flexibly added into the temporal
dimension to reassess the relative value of various moments. The main
purpose of AC-GGN is to learn a mapping function f using the topological
structure and the recorded time series, allowing for the prediction of the

That is to say, AC-GGN is good at capturing patterns and dependencies of

3.2.2. GCN on spatial dependency modeling
As a highly innovative and impactful development in deep learning,
GCN opens a new opportunity to understand the graph-structure data. It
mainly builds a filter in the Fourier domain for learning a function of
dynamic features on a graph G=(V, E). Herein, GCN directly operates on
the graph structure to take advantage of the structural information,
where each monitoring point is regarded as a node and the whole
monitoring site is treated as a graph. It is assumed that the connected
nodes have a cause-and-effect relation to a certain extent, thereby
allowing the convolutional extraction of feature information from the
node itself and its corresponding neighbors. The novel neural network
utilizes two inputs for its operation. One is the adjacency matrix
A ∈ RN×N(N stands for the number of nodes) derived from the GC test,
which offers a representative description of the graph structure in a
matrix form. Only two elements, including 0 and 1, are in the adjacency
matrix. Element 0 means no links between nodes, while element 1 needs
to connect two nodes. The other is the feature matrix X ∈ RN×D con­
taining time-series information of each monitoring point (D denotes the
number of features of each node).
As an example, the typical structure of GCN with two convolutional
layers is visualized in Fig. 3, which contains the tasks of transmission,
aggregation, and nonlinear transformation. In this architecture, each
node has access to information from its connected nodes, which is
subsequently aggregated and averaged out at each layer. Afterward, the
resulting vector goes through a dense neural network layer, where a
non-linear activation function is applied to derive a new vector repre­
sentation. The basic rule of GCN can be mathematically expressed by a
propagation process in Eq. (7), where the (l + 1)th layer of information
for each node can be obtained from its neighbor nodes (including itself)
in the lth layer.

3.2.3. GRU with attention mechanism on temporal dependence modeling
The output of GCN is then delivered to GRU to learn temporal fea­
tures on each monitoring point. This is a crucial step in predicting
ground settlement, which exhibits temporal dynamic behavior. After
that, an attention mechanism module is concatenated with GRU to show
the merits of improving both the prediction accuracy and model inter­
pretability. The combination of GRU and attention mechanism is ex­
pected to extract the state of ground settlement in different monitoring
points even from several steps before the actual time step. This ability to
capture the temporal aspects of time-series data is highly beneficial for
studying complex and dynamic systems.
(1) GRU structure
GRU shares a similar principle with LSTM, which incorporates a
gating mechanism to control the flow of information and memorize
long-term time series as much as possible. Through selective retention
and erasure of relevant and irrelevant data, the GRU enables effective
information propagation across long chains for accurate prediction. The
reason for choosing GRU is its superiority of adaptively capturing de­
pendencies from different time scales, making it an ideal choice for time
series forecasting. Compared to LSTM, the GRU model owns a smaller
number of parameters to avoid the overfitting issue to a certain extent.
Meanwhile, it can achieve comparable performance with the advantages
of high computing efficiency and low memory consumption.
The internal structure of the GRU is depicted in Fig. 4, which is made
up of a reset gate rt and an update gate ut to correspond to the hidden
state of each unit. To be more specific, the reset gate rt determines the
amount of information from the previous hidden state to forget. It helps
to discard historical information that is irrelevant to the future state. The
update gate ut controls how much information from the previous hidden
state is passed along to the current hidden state. It serves as an infor­
mation copy process to eliminate the risk of gradient vanishing. Outputs
of two gates are calculated by two fully connected layers with a sigmoid
activation function.

(2) Attention mechanism
As a main frontier in deep learning, the attention mechanism is an
evolution of the encoder-decoder model, where an encoder generates an
attention vector from the input and a decoder takes the encoder output
to produce a hidden state. In particular, the encoder part employs a
scoring function to assign varying levels of importance to the hidden
state of each step based on the output from the decoder. The primary
objective of the attention mechanism is to selectively focus on valuable
features while disregarding irrelevant information, thereby optimizing
the deep neural network’s performance. As a result, the most beneficial
data embedded in the available resources can be easily found, which is
especially useful to avoid forgetting the earlier parts of a sequence and
improve prediction performance in the presence of long input
sequences.

3.3.2. Data augmentation
Since the precision of deep learning is greatly influenced by the
quality, quantity, and contextual meaning of training data, the limited
size of the monitoring dataset in the engineering practice will, unfor­
tunately, lower the prediction ability of the developed AC-GGN. To
tackle this challenge, a promising data-space solution called data
augmentation is worthy of consideration, which can be tailored to suit
various purposes and has gained significant popularity across a wide
range of domains, such as image classification (Han et al., 2018; Zheng
et al., 2020), object detection (Bosquet et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023;
Wang et al., 2021). Moreover, data augmentation has been successfully
applied in time-series tasks, like voice recognition (Nugroho et al., 2022;
Qian et al., 2019) and autonomous vehicles (Khosravian et al., 2021;
Zhang et al., 2022). Typically, data augmentation is a process of artifi­
cially expanding the size of the training set using synthetic patterns,
where modified copies of a using existing data are generated based on
the existing data. It has proven useful in improving performance and
generalization when training neural network models. Also, it helps to
avoid overfitting the trained data.
It is known that the choice of data augmentation techniques depends
on the specific application purposes. For example, image recognition
tasks prefer geometric transformations like flipping, cropping, and
rotation, while the effectiveness of text-based applications comes from
augmentation algorithms like paraphrasing, back-translation, and add­
ing synonyms. Unlike image or text data, time series data augmentation
places a stronger emphasis on the so-called temporal dependency, where
the time series data can be transformed into the frequency and time­
–frequency domains. Under such circumstances, Abbasimehr et al.
(Abbasimehr et al., 2022) employed the decomposition-based augmen­
tation for COVID-19 time series forecasting by adjusting weights for
reminder, trend, and seasonality. For multivariate time series, Kamycki
et al. (Kamycki et al., 2019) generated synthetic time-series in the
warped space between time-series using a suboptimal alignment and
enriched the training dataset. This novel method considers the poten­
tially complex dynamics of multidimensional features across time and
outperforms related augmentation algorithms in terms of classification
accuracy. However, these methods, to the best of our knowledge, have
never been used to augment time series in predicting excavation
monitoring data. By introducing greater diversity and complexity into
the data, data augmentation can provide a more robust training dataset,
which helps improve the predictive performance of monitoring data.
This is crucial for accurately forecasting the behavior and risks associ­
ated with excavations.
Inspired by the success in diverse fields, three easy-implemented
techniques relying on simple transformation, including jittering,
scaling, and magnitude wrap, are directly operated on the existing data
samples to generate new and diverse instances for the training set. Only
the values of each element are modified, while the time steps are kept
constant (Li et al., 2022). To be more specific, jittering refers to the idea
of adding small amounts of noise to data. Scaling aims to randomly pick
the short size of a sequence within a dimension range. Magnitude wrap,
on the other hand, alters the magnitude of each sample in a signal by
convolving a smooth curve that varies around one with the data win­
dow. To illustrate the effectiveness of these three data augmentation
techniques in dealing with limited training data, results are demon­
strated in Fig. 5, where jittering adds Gaussian noise j ∈ N(0, 0.03) to the
training set, scaling multiplies a random variable s ∈ N(1, 0.1) with the
training set, and magnitude wrap multiplies a cubic spline curve with
four knots interpolating the random variable m ∈ N(1, 0.2) with the
training set. It is observed that these three data augmentation methods
can artificially increase the length of sequences in different ways.
3.3.3. Sobol sensitivity analysis
The main idea underlying the established AC-GGN model is to
aggregate the information of neighboring nodes to facilitate non-linear
changes and enable global spatial feature extraction. Based on this,
the global sensitivity analysis (GSA) can be directly deployed to the ACGGN model to provide additional insights into the spatial distribution of
outputs. In this research, GSA is utilized to quantify the extent to which
the evolution of ground settlement at a particular monitoring point
impacts other points. As anticipated, GSA enables the revelation of the
mutual influence between various monitoring points. Meanwhile, the
influence degree of monitoring points can be ranked using the
sensitivity-weighted sum. Therefore, GSA also paves the way to improve
the interpretability of deep learning.
Among various GSA methods, Sobol based on variance is well-suited
to explain complex deep learning models (Fu and Zhang, 2021). It
adopts the variance decomposition to identify the contribution of each
uncertain input to the unconditional output variance of a model, which
can be defined by two sensitivity indices, including the first-order
sensitivity in Eq. (19) and the total sensitivity in Eq. (20). For one
thing, first-order sensitivity only reflects the uncertain influence of each
parameter to the total variance. For another, the total sensitivity ex­
presses the uncertainty influence of each parameter and the interaction
influence of this parameter with other parameters.
