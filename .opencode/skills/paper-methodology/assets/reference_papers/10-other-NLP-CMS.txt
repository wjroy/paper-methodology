3. Research methodology

This section outlines the research methodology employed to investigate the efficacy of pre-trained deep learning models on domain-specific tasks within the CMS domain. The central premise of this study is that deep learning models, initially pre-trained on a general corpus as shown in Fig. 1a, will benefit from additional pre-training on a CMS-specific domain corpus.

This additional pre-training phase, depicted in Fig. 1b, is hypothesized to enhance the models' comprehension of domain-specific nuances, thereby optimizing their performance in downstream NLP tasks, a.k.a. CMS-related NLP tasks such as TC and NER, without necessitating increased manual annotation efforts. The overall procedure for pre-training and fine-tuning is shown in Fig. 1b and c.

The methodology of this research is further detailed through the workflow depicted in Fig. 2. The proposed workflow consists of three parts:
(1) Domain corpora development: This phase involves the development of four unique domain corpora, each subject to differing data cleaning and pre-processing techniques. Further details of this stage can be found in Section 4.1.
(2) Pre-train PLMs on domain corpora - a detailed exposition of this phase is available in Section 3.1.
(3) The finetuning of pre-trained domain models - this phase encompasses the development and assessment of several DL models of varying architectural designs for TC and NER tasks. A more extensive explanation of this step is provided in Section 3.2.

The entirety of the workflow is implemented in Python 3.9 and leverages a multitude of Python packages including Transformers, PyTorch, Selenium, Pandas, Pickle, Sklearn, Matplotlib, and Numpy, to facilitate the development of our models.

The research presented herein employs two prominent PLMs: the BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) model, as conceptualized by Devlin et al. [13], and the RoBERTa (A Robustly Optimized BERT Pretraining Approach) model, developed by Liu et al. [30]. Both models are built upon the innovative architecture of the Transformer encoder, which is illustrated in Fig. 3a. Specifically, the encoding procedure of Transformer encoder is shown in Fig. 3b, which will be discussed in detail in Section 3.1.1.

The RoBERTa model represents a sophisticated evolution of the original BERT architecture, distinguished by several key enhancements. These include an extended period of training, larger batch sizes during this phase, and a broader spectrum of training data. Notably, RoBERTa omits the next sentence prediction task, a feature of its predecessor, in favor of training on extended sequence lengths. Additionally, it incorporates a dynamic approach to altering the masking pattern employed in the training data, further refining the learning process.

3.1. Pretrain PLM on domain corpus

The series of steps constituting the pre-training procedure, as diagrammatically represented in the middle section of Fig. 2, will be elucidated in sequential order in the following Section 3.1.1, 3.1.3, and 3.1.4.

3.1.1. Word embedding methods

Word embeddings serve as a method for encoding semantic information in words, where learned representations of text enable words with analogous meanings to exhibit similar representations. Essentially, this is a form of word vectorization, a technique to convert textual data into a numerical form that can be comprehended by a machine.

The quality of these word embeddings frequently exerts a substantial impact on the performance of downstream NLP tasks. In the context of this research, the WordPiece algorithm, as employed by both BERT and RoBERTa models, is utilized for tokenization and embedding generation. This algorithm breaks down words into subword units, thereby handling out-of-vocabulary words effectively and capturing morphological nuances within the CMS domain.

The Transformer encoder architecture, which forms the backbone of both BERT and RoBERTa, processes these embeddings through multiple layers of self-attention mechanisms. Each layer applies multi-head self-attention, allowing the model to weigh the importance of different words in the context of each other, followed by position-wise feed-forward networks. The self-attention mechanism computes attention scores between all pairs of positions in the input sequence, enabling the model to capture long-range dependencies and contextual relationships within construction management texts.

The mathematical formulation of the self-attention mechanism can be expressed as:

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QK^T / √d_k)V

where Q represents the query matrix, K the key matrix, V the value matrix, and d_k the dimension of the key vectors. This scaled dot-product attention allows the model to focus on relevant parts of the input sequence when encoding each word.

Furthermore, the Transformer employs positional encodings to inject information about the position of tokens in the sequence, as the self-attention mechanism itself is position-invariant. These encodings use sine and cosine functions of different frequencies:

PE(pos, 2i) = sin(pos / 10000^(2i/d_model))
PE(pos, 2i+1) = cos(pos / 10000^(2i/d_model))

where pos is the position and i is the dimension. This allows the model to learn relative positions effectively, which is crucial for understanding the sequential nature of construction management documents.

3.1.2. Sentence concatenation

In their study, Liu et al. [30] posited that the utilization of individual sentences during pre-training could negatively impact performance on downstream tasks. They hypothesized this detriment to be the result of the model's incapacity to learn long-range dependencies from isolated sentences. Furthermore, it is observed that the sentences contained within the CMS domain dataset exhibit an average length of approximately 30.

Given this context, we presume that concatenating sentences to the maximum token length accepted by a pre-trained model (512 for BERT) might significantly enhance the training speed. Thus, we plan to conduct a comprehensive evaluation wherein the pre-training of the PLM is carried out on the CMS dataset both with and without sentence concatenation. Subsequently, we aim to compare the resultant outcomes to discern the impact of sentence concatenation on the model's performance and training efficiency.

Following data cleaning, sentence concatenation is executed in the following procedural steps:
(1) Tokenize all sentences to yield a list of individual tokens.
(2) Concatenate tokens into a single string, adhering to their original sequential order.
(3) When the aggregate token count within the string surpasses the predefined maximum token length acceptable for the PLM, the string comprising the tokens that precede the sentence that exceeded the limit is appended as an independent data point.

This systematic approach allows us to efficiently concatenate whole sentences while maintaining compliance with the constraints of the PLM's maximum token capacity.

3.1.3. Dynamic masked language modeling

In order to pre-train a PLM on domain-specific corpora, we initiate by randomly masking a certain percentage of the input tokens. This process, known as Masked Language Modeling (MLM), involves obscuring parts of the input text and training the model to predict these masked tokens.

Subsequently, these masked tokens are predicted using the contextual information provided by the surrounding unmasked tokens. This approach forces the model to develop a deep understanding of language structure and semantics, as it must infer the missing information based on context rather than relying solely on local co-occurrence patterns.

The masking strategy follows the approach originally proposed by Devlin et al. [13], where 15% of all WordPiece tokens in each sequence are selected for masking. Of these selected tokens:
- 80% are replaced with the [MASK] token
- 10% are replaced with a random token
- 10% remain unchanged

This random replacement strategy prevents the model from simply memorizing the masked positions and encourages it to maintain a distributed contextual representation for every input token.

The model is trained to minimize the cross-entropy loss between the predicted probability distribution over the vocabulary and the actual identity of the masked tokens:

L_MLM = -Σi∈M log P(xi | x̃)

where M represents the set of masked positions, xi is the original token at position i, and x̃ represents the partially masked input sequence.

During the pre-training phase, the model parameters are updated iteratively over multiple epochs using the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate warmup schedule. The learning rate is gradually increased from 0 to a peak value during the warmup period, then decayed linearly. This scheduling helps stabilize training in the early stages and allows for finer convergence as training progresses.

The batch size, number of training steps, and other hyperparameters are configured based on computational resources and the size of the domain corpus. Gradient accumulation is employed when necessary to achieve effective batch sizes that exceed GPU memory constraints.

3.2. Fine-tuning for downstream tasks

After domain-specific pre-training, the resulting models are fine-tuned on specific downstream tasks including text classification (TC) and named entity recognition (NER). The fine-tuning process involves adding task-specific output layers on top of the pre-trained Transformer encoder and training the entire model end-to-end on labeled task-specific data.

For text classification, a simple linear layer is added on top of the [CLS] token representation, followed by a softmax activation to produce class probabilities:

P(y | x) = softmax(W_CLS · h_CLS + b_CLS)

where h_CLS is the hidden state corresponding to the [CLS] token, and W_CLS and b_CLS are learnable parameters.

For named entity recognition, a linear layer is applied to each token's hidden state to produce tag scores:

P(y_i | x) = softmax(W_NER · h_i + b_NER)

where h_i is the hidden state for token i. The model is trained using conditional random field (CRF) loss to ensure valid tag sequences.

The fine-tuning process uses a smaller learning rate than pre-training to preserve the knowledge encoded in the pre-trained weights while adapting to task-specific patterns. Early stopping based on validation performance prevents overfitting.
