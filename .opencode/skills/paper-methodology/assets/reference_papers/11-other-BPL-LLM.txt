3 Methodology  3.1 Research Framework  This study employs two methodologies: BPL and LLM. The research framework is delineated in Fig. 2, with the core procedural steps outlined below, and detailed elaborations provided in the subsections that follow.  Step 1: Identify and evaluate risks to obtain a risk register for a selected project.  Step 2: Design a Generative Pre-trained Transformer (GPT) agent for probability estimation by interacting with the GPT builder. Developed to emulate humanlike learning capabilities, BPL focuses on how humans can effectively generalize from limited examples. In BPL, learning is conceptualized as a form of program synthesis, aiming to deduce an underlying program that generates observed data. It is then interpreted as the process of belief revision based on new evidence. The fundamental equation in BPL is:P(Program|Data) = P(Program)P(Program)  P(Data) , #(1)  where P(Program|Data) is the posterior probability of a program given the data, which represents the updated belief about the distribution of programs; P(Program) is the likelihood of the data given a specific program, which quantifies how likely the observed data is given a particular program; P(Program) is the prior probability of a program, which represents our beliefs about the distribution of programs before observing any data; and P(Data) is the evidence or marginal likelihood, which normalizes the probability distribution.  Further to this, a transformed illustration in this study of probability estimation is:  P(Mitigation) = P(Risk event)P(Risk event)  P(Mitigation) , #(2)#  where the risk event is the program and the mitigation is the data. Thus, the key technique is to assume every risk event as a program corresponding to a probability distribution, and then derive the parameters via machine learning utilizing the Bayesian approach.  Following this, it is essential to define the data distributions with appropriate assumptions. For prior distribution P(Risk event), it may be posited that certain patterns or shifts occur in one project. The Beta distribution frequently serves as a suitable choice in such instances due to its flexibility, governed by its two parameters, α and β, which can vary substantially across different scenarios (Fig. 6). The Beta distribution is chosen due to its well-known flexibility in modeling bounded probability values (i.e., between 0 and 1), a key requirement in estimating risk event likelihoods. Furthermore, the Beta distribution offers practical advantages over other flexible families (e.g., Pearson distribution systems, Johnson distribution systems) in terms of mathematical tractability and interpretability. Its parameters, α and β, are easily updated through Bayesian inference, and its familiarity among risk analysis practitioners supports clearer communication and implementation. This pipeline eliminates any ambiguity in how probabilities are derived and transforms the GPT agent from a black-box generator into a constrained semantic extractor embedded within a transparent mathematical system.  3.6 Step 5: Training and Validation  To strengthen the generalizability of the proposed GPT–Bayesian framework, we train and validate it by collecting 300 data entries of risk events. They are obtained from Google search by the keywords “risk register” and “construction”; only those with clear risk events, mitigations, and probabilities are included. Finally, 69 qualified risk registers are collected, yielding 300 data entries. This dataset is randomly partitioned into 70% for training and 30% for testing.  During training, the GPT Bayesian Probability Estimator is exposed to the 210 risk events (70% of the data) to calibrate prompt structures, improve semantic-to-evidence extraction consistency, and optimize the conversion of textual risk narratives into structured Bayesian evidence (k, n, p0). No model weights are modified; instead, the training phase refines rule-based constraints to ensure accurate, stable outputs and appropriate interpretation of mitigation effects.  For validation, the remaining 90 risk events (30% of the data) are processed without any further prompt tuning, and model performance is evaluated using the following criteria:  i. Semantic Extraction Accuracy: Agreement between GPT-inferred probabilities and published benchmark probabilities measured by Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE).  ii. Posterior Consistency: Stability of Beta–Binomial posterior means under repeated trials and small perturbations of (k, n).  iii. Calibration Reliability: Comparison of predicted probabilities with empirical frequencies using Brier score and reliability diagrams assessing over- or underconfidence.  This process ends once the authors evaluate a reasonable output. The combined criteria confirm whether the GPT–Bayesian estimator (1) interprets risk descriptions consistently, (2) produces mathematically coherent posterior probabilities, and (3) generalizes beyond the specific structure of the training registers.
