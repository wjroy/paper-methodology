3. Methodology
The knowledge graph is applied to reflect BHR response process and
understand the intrinsic and interactive relationships in the BHR
ontology. This study proposes an automated knowledge graph con­
struction method empowered by LLMs, and the construction steps are
shown in Fig. 1.
3.1. Crawling and preprocessing of multi-modal unstructured data
Structured data can enhance the efficiency of building knowledge
graphs because it often inherently includes ontologies and entities [55].
However, in most cases, data exists in an unstructured form [56],
especially in emerging fields where structured data may not be readily
available. Hence, extracting information from unstructured data to
construct knowledge graphs has become an important area of research.
Unstructured data, such as news and reports, reflects significant societal
issues and serves as not only an important source of knowledge provided
by the public but also a valuable channel for information acquisition
[57]. Due to the emerging nature of the BHR field examined in this
study, there is a lack of rich, structured data to support it. Therefore, this
study focuses on collecting unstructured data related to BHR to support
the construction of the knowledge graph. This study utilizes Python and
APIFY [58] to crawl multi-modal data, including news, images, and
videos, as our data sources from social media for constructing knowl­
edge graphs.
For the news text data, APIFY was directly used to crawl the main
body of the news, and preprocessing tasks such as deduplication,
denoising, and screening were carried out on it. For the image data, a
crawler was used to extract the first image of the news, and ChatGPT-4o
was employed to generate descriptive information based on the features
and themes of the image, thus converting the information contained in
the image into text. For the video data, the media API was used to obtain
all the news within the screening time range and conduct manual
screening. Then, OCR (Optical Character Recognition) and speech
recognition tools were utilized to extract the subtitle information,
transforming the video data into text. The process of obtaining and
processing the multimodal data in this paper is shown in Fig. 2.
3.2. Ontology design
The ontology is the foundation for constructing a knowledge graph,
as it defines the standards for knowledge sharing within a specific field
[59]. It encompasses the field’s fundamental concepts and their collec­
tions, as well as the definitions of concept relations and extension rules.
This study employs a combination of top-down and bottom-up methods
for BHR ontology design. The principle for construction is to propose the
top-level design of the ontology based on the knowledge concept of BHR
(Top-down) and then assist in the construction of the hierarchical
structure of the ontology by building a corpus with existing data (Bot­
tom-up). This approach combines theoretical underpinnings with datadriven techniques, ensuring the ontology is both grounded in expert
knowledge and sensitive to the specific nuances within the corpus. It
enhances the accuracy and comprehensiveness of the knowledge graph,
reinforcing its utility as a robust tool to analyze and address complex
issues within the BHR field.

3.2.1. Top-down method
The top-down approach begins with an analysis of the concept of
BHR. At the “building health” level, the health of the building and its
occupants is considered a holistic entity, referred to as the building
system in this study. “Resilience” occurs only when the building system
is faced with disturbances and threats, during which the health state of
the building system will change.
The entire process of BHR mainly consists of three stages. Firstly,
there is the preparation stage: the health status of the building system
can be automatically maintained and improved, remaining at a normal
level and being unaffected by external disturbances and threats. The
resistance stage refers to the situation where, when the building system
faces external disturbances, its health status is affected and reaches the
lowest point of its health status at the last moment of this stage. In this
stage, the disaster-inducing factors and the affected objects are the key
focuses. The recovery stage is when the health status of the building
system begins to improve until it is fully restored, and the external threat
disturbances gradually disappear. In this stage, emergency response
measures and other measures (such as post-disaster repair measures)
need to be taken to restore the health status of the building to the normal
level.
The entire BHR cycle has similarities with the risk disaster ontology
[40]. To describe the BHR ontology through a hierarchical structure,
according to the connotation of the BHR life cycle, the upper framework
of BHR should include the following parts: internal or external distur­
bances to the building, changes in the health status of the building and
its occupants, factors influencing the change in health status of in­
habitants, and measures taken to cope with disturbances to restore the
normal functions of the building.
3.2.2. Bottom-up method
This study applies topic modeling techniques to categorize the con­
ceptual characteristics of BHR and lists the important terminology in
BHR to assist in the tiered construction of the BHR ontology. Ontology
construction requires a strong semantic expressive capacity, capable of
eliminating semantic differences between concepts at different levels
[59]. In addition to the top-down BHR ontology construction based on
concept analysis, it’s necessary to extract the various concepts repre­
sented in the BHR field knowledge to assist in the decomposition of the
ontology’s hierarchical structure. Because BHR is an emerging field, no
scholar has yet structured the decomposition of BHR’s knowledge
ontology.
In order to accurately extract BHR-related concepts from unstruc­
tured text, this study employs the ChatGPT-empowered BERTopic [27]
for knowledge extraction. The BERTopic model [60] is developed based
on Top2Vec, utilizing a word embedding-based topic word extraction
algorithm. This algorithm integrates the latest NLP techniques and,
compared to traditional topic models like LDA (Latent Dirichlet Allo­
cation), it plays a more effective role in mining the deep semantic rep­
resentations of texts, enabling the extraction of more representative
topic words. As a representative product of LLMs, ChatGPT excels at
numerous NLP tasks, such as translation, writing, etc. Although
ChatGPT’s overall development is not mature, with a tendency toward
“hallucination” in its outputs, it has already demonstrated excellent
performance in text summarization [61]. Combining ChatGPT with
BERTopic can address some of these limitations. For instance, the
BERTopic model requires high-quality input texts and has limited
capability in analyzing overly lengthy texts. However, a ChatGPTempowered BERTopic model can extract topic words more compre­
hensively and accurately. The steps of the ChatGPT-empowered BER­
Topic model are shown in Fig. 3.
(1). Document Embedding. Input textual data is embedded into a lan­
guage that a computer can understand. To ensure the embedding
vectors retain the original semantic features of the text as much as
possible, the “all-MiniLM-L6-v2” model pre-trained by Microsoft
is used. This model is a pre-trained large language model, excels
in semantic understanding, making it suitable for processing
multimodal data, which enables it to better capture the semantic
relationships among concepts related to BHR.
(2). Dimensionality Reduction and Clustering. Generally, the dimen­
sionality of text data after embedding is high. BERTopic employs
the “UMAP” algorithm to reduce the dimensionality of vectors. Its
superiority in preserving data structure and distribution allows
for clearer visualization of high-dimensional datasets, facilitating
subsequent analysis. The “HDBSCAN” algorithm was chosen for
clustering, discovering potential topics through textual vector
features. Due to its low sensitivity to noise, it can effectively
identify complex cluster structures in real-world data, thereby
improving clustering accuracy [62].
(3). Tokenization and Topic Assessment. The “CounterVectorizer” was
used because of its simplicity and high efficiency, its ability to
perform word frequency statistics, its excellent compatibility
with other models, and its strong customizability. It can quickly
tokenize the topics clustered by BERTopic and assist in the
extraction of key information and subsequent analysis during the
construction of the knowledge graph of BHR. “c-TF-IDF” was used
(a variant of TF-IDF) to evaluate the importance of each word
within each HDBSCAN cluster, selecting representative words for
each topic for thematic representation. This algorithm empha­
sizes keywords with high frequencies within specific classes
rather than across all documents, aligning well with the feature
extraction requirements of our knowledge graph centered on
building health resilience.

(4). Topic Extraction. By inputting topics and representative docu­
ments, prompts are constructed, and the ChatGPT-4o API is uti­

final compilation of a topic word library.
Finally, the top-level concepts of BHR are analyzed using a top-down
approach, while the bottom-up approach is employed to hierarchically
decompose BHR knowledge. The results of these two methods are then
integrated and refined through mutual supplementation and matching
of concepts and subcategories, culminating in the final BHR ontology.
3.3. Llms for entity recognition and relation extraction
After constructing the ontology, it is necessary to identify the entities
corresponding to the concepts as well as the relations between these
entities. However, in an emerging field, extracting entities and re­
lationships from unstructured text using traditional identification
methods is complex and requires extensive manual annotation. LLMs
exhibit good performance in text generation and question-answering
tasks. There are many LLMs in today’s market, among them, ChatGPT
is a popular AI tool proposed by OpenAI [63]. ChatGPT operates on a
generative artificial intelligence-based question-and-answer system, and
it possesses significant abilities in translation, creating stories, debug­
ging code, and other aspects [64]. Particularly regarding summarization
capabilities based on text, many scholars have demonstrated that
ChatGPT performs well in analyzing and understanding natural lan­
guage and summarizing the main points according to semantic relations
[61,65].
Constructing a knowledge graph from unstructured text is exceed­
ingly complex, requiring extensive manual annotation of entities and
relationships. Even semi-automatic supervised algorithms like BILSTMCRF and BERT-BILSTM-CRF for NER need certain annotated data. This
process can consume considerable human effort. Despite some “hallu­
cination”, ChatGPT-4o, as a generative AI tool, is highly accredited for
its capacity to extract knowledge from unstructured text automatically
[66]. ChatGPT-4o excels in text summarization and semantic analysis
due to its advanced neural architecture, which enables deeper contex­
tual comprehension, improved coherence, and more accurate extraction
of key information. Its ability to understand nuances, identify relation­
ships between concepts, and generate concise yet comprehensive sum­
maries makes it highly effective for tasks requiring precise language
processing [67]. Additionally, its enhanced training data and optimi­
zation techniques contribute to better generalization across diverse
topics, ensuring more relevant and insightful responses. Using ChatGPT-
4o for named entity and relationship recognition not only greatly saves
on human annotation efforts but also better understands the contextual
semantic relationships in any field’s unstructured text. It is not con­
strained by a specific set of concepts, which is an effective ability that
pre-trained NLP models cannot achieve [68].
Utilizing the ChatGPT-4o application programming interface (API)
to summarize unstructured knowledge within unstructured texts allows
for the refinement and structuring of this knowledge. This article le­
verages ChatGPT-4o to propose a model for entity and relationship
extraction based on feedback checking. The specific content of the
model is shown in Fig. 4.
Store the processed multi-modal data in text form and employ
prompt engineering to enable ChatGPT to automatically extract triples
from the text data. For each text document, ChatGPT follows these steps:
(1) Triple Extraction Prompt Engineering: 1) Ontology framework
learning: Start by inputting the ontology framework. This ensures
that the recognized entities and relationships fall within the
definitions provided by the ontology. 2) Annotation case
learning. Using three real news articles as examples, manually
annotate all triples. These examples will serve as cases for
ChatGPT to learn from. 3) Automated Triples Extraction:
ChatGPT automatically extracts triples using the developed
prompt engineering strategy. The triples should then be saved in
JSON format.
(2) FCM Prompt Engineering: Use the initial triple extraction results
alongside the original document as inputs to construct prompts.
1) Triples supplement: This helps in reprocessing the triples.
Begin by comparing the initial triple results with the original
document to check for any omissions. 2) Semantic consistency
checking. Verify the relationships between entities and concepts
using semantic checks to ensure accuracy. 3) Deletion of
Redundancy. Delete any triples that have the same overall
meaning to eliminate redundancy. 4) Similar Entities Merging.
Further, filter the extracted entities by checking their semantic
similarity and consistency, and merge the entities with the same
semantics. By following these steps, the process ensures that the
extraction and refinement of triples are both systematic and ac­
curate, resulting in a well-structured and reliable triple data set.
3.4. In-context learning prompts
To enhance LLMs’ reasoning abilities of entities and relations
extraction for BHR, especially, this study employs ICL-based prompts to
train the LLMs. ICL has been popularized as a method for learning tasks
using LLMs in GPT-3. Essentially, it uses a pre-trained language model to
conditionally generate probabilistic distributions based on given
examples [69]. In this study, the baseline prompt, few-shot prompt, and
Chain of Thought (CoT) prompt are integrated into ChatGPT-4o based
on ICL [50], aiming to pre-train ChatGPT-4o’s reasoning steps, thereby
refining the underlying logic for inferring entity relationship triples of
BHR.

extraction such as triples formed by nodes, node types, and edges,
as well as a set of constraints for the extraction process. Addi­
tionally, the output format is also defined.
(2) Few-shot Prompt. Fig. 5B shows the content of the few-shot
