3. Methodology

The knowledge graph is applied to reflect BHR response process and understand the intrinsic and interactive relationships in the BHR ontology. This study proposes an automated knowledge graph construction method empowered by LLMs, and the construction steps are shown in Fig. 1.

3.1. Crawling and preprocessing of multi-modal unstructured data

Structured data can enhance the efficiency of building knowledge graphs because it often inherently includes ontologies and entities [55]. However, in most cases, data exists in an unstructured form [56], especially in emerging fields where structured data may not be readily available. Hence, extracting information from unstructured data to construct knowledge graphs has become an important area of research.

Unstructured data, such as news and reports, reflects significant societal issues and serves as not only an important source of knowledge provided by the public but also a valuable channel for information acquisition [57]. Due to the emerging nature of the BHR field examined in this study, there is a lack of rich, structured data to support it. Therefore, this study focuses on collecting unstructured data related to BHR to support the construction of the knowledge graph. This study utilizes Python and APIFY [58] to crawl multi-modal data, including news, images, and videos, as our data sources from social media for constructing knowledge graphs.

For the news text data, APIFY was directly used to crawl the main body of the news, and preprocessing tasks such as deduplication, denoising, and screening were carried out on it. For the image data, a crawler was used to extract the first image of the news, and ChatGPT-4o was employed to generate descriptive information based on the features and themes of the image, thus converting the information contained in the image into text. For the video data, the media API was used to obtain all the news within the screening time range and conduct manual screening. Then, OCR (Optical Character Recognition) and speech recognition tools were utilized to extract the subtitle information, transforming the video data into text. The process of obtaining and processing the multimodal data in this paper is shown in Fig. 2.

3.2. Ontology design

The ontology is the foundation for constructing a knowledge graph, as it defines the standards for knowledge sharing within a specific field [59]. It encompasses the field's fundamental concepts and their collections, as well as the definitions of concept relations and extension rules. This study employs a combination of top-down and bottom-up methods for BHR ontology design.

The principle for construction is to propose the top-level design of the ontology based on the knowledge concept of BHR (Top-down) and then assist in the construction of the hierarchical structure of the ontology by building a corpus with existing data (Bottom-up). This approach combines theoretical underpinnings with data-driven techniques, ensuring the ontology is both grounded in expert knowledge and sensitive to the specific nuances within the corpus. It enhances the accuracy and comprehensiveness of the knowledge graph, reinforcing its utility as a robust tool to analyze and address complex issues within the BHR field.

O_BHR = {KG_concept, KG_relations, KG_property, KG_entity}

While KG_concept represents the collection of all relevant knowledge concepts under the proposed topic, such as the disturbance of a rainstorm in BHR, including the definition of BHR ontology knowledge and other extracted topics; KG_relations represents the semantic relationships between BHR knowledge under the disturbance of a rainstorm, such as hierarchical relationships and parallel relationships; KG_property describes the attributes of BHR knowledge under the disturbance of a rainstorm, usually represented as text strings and numbers, as well as media images; KG_entity represents a specific instance of BHR knowledge under the disturbance of a rainstorm, such as black rain, building collapse, etc.

3.2.1. Top-down method

The top-down approach begins with an analysis of the concept of BHR. At the "building health" level, the health of the building and its occupants is considered a holistic entity, referred to as the building system in this study. "Resilience" occurs only when the building system is faced with disturbances and threats, during which the health state of the building system will change.

The entire process of BHR mainly consists of three stages. Firstly, there is the preparation stage: the health status of the building system can be automatically maintained and improved, remaining at a normal level and being unaffected by external disturbances and threats.

The resistance stage refers to the situation where, when the building system faces external disturbances, its health status is affected and reaches the lowest point of its health status at the last moment of this stage. In this stage, the disaster-inducing factors and the affected objects are the key focuses.

The recovery stage is when the health status of the building system begins to improve until it is fully restored, and the external threat disturbances gradually disappear. In this stage, emergency response measures and other measures (such as post-disaster repair measures) need to be taken to restore the health status of the building to the normal level.

The entire BHR cycle has similarities with the risk disaster ontology [40]. To describe the BHR ontology through a hierarchical structure, according to the connotation of the BHR life cycle, the upper framework of BHR should include the following parts: internal or external disturbances to the building, changes in the health status of the building and its occupants, factors influencing the change in health status of inhabitants, and measures taken to cope with disturbances to restore the normal functions of the building.

3.2.2. Bottom-up method

This study applies topic modeling techniques to categorize the conceptual characteristics of BHR and lists the important terminology in BHR to assist in the tiered construction of the BHR ontology. Ontology construction requires a strong semantic expressive capacity, capable of eliminating semantic differences between concepts at different levels [59].

In addition to the top-down BHR ontology construction based on concept analysis, it's necessary to extract the various concepts represented in the BHR field knowledge to assist in the decomposition of the ontology's hierarchical structure. Because BHR is an emerging field, no scholar has yet structured the decomposition of BHR's knowledge ontology.

In order to accurately extract BHR-related concepts from unstructured text, this study employs the ChatGPT-empowered BERTopic [27] for knowledge extraction. The BERTopic model [60] is developed based on Top2Vec, utilizing a word embedding-based topic word extraction algorithm. This algorithm integrates the latest NLP techniques and, compared to traditional topic models like LDA (Latent Dirichlet Allocation), it plays a more effective role in mining the deep semantic representations of texts, enabling the extraction of more representative topic words.

As a representative product of LLMs, ChatGPT excels at numerous NLP tasks, such as translation, writing, etc. Although ChatGPT's overall development is not mature, with a tendency toward "hallucination" in its outputs, it has already demonstrated excellent performance in text summarization [61]. Combining ChatGPT with BERTopic can address some of these limitations. For instance, the BERTopic model requires high-quality input texts and has limited capability in analyzing overly lengthy texts. However, a ChatGPT-empowered BERTopic model can extract topic words more comprehensively and accurately. The steps of the ChatGPT-empowered BERTopic model are shown in Fig. 3.

(1) Document Embedding

Input textual data is embedded into a language that a computer can understand. To ensure the embedding vectors retain the original semantic features of the text as much as possible, the "all-MiniLM-L6-v2" model pre-trained by Microsoft is used. This model is a pre-trained large language model, excels in semantic understanding, making it suitable for processing multimodal data, which enables it to better capture the semantic relationships among concepts related to BHR.

(2) Dimensionality Reduction and Clustering

Generally, the dimensionality of text data after embedding is high. BERTopic employs the "UMAP" algorithm to reduce the dimensionality of vectors. Its superiority in preserving data structure and distribution allows for clearer visualization of high-dimensional datasets, facilitating subsequent analysis. The "HDBSCAN" algorithm was chosen for clustering, discovering potential topics through textual vector features. Due to its low sensitivity to noise, it can effectively identify complex cluster structures in real-world data, thereby improving clustering accuracy [62].

(3) Tokenization and Topic Assessment

The "CounterVectorizer" was used because of its simplicity and high efficiency, its ability to perform word frequency statistics, its excellent compatibility with other models, and its strong customizability. It can quickly tokenize the topics clustered by BERTopic and assist in the extraction of key information and subsequent analysis during the construction of the knowledge graph of BHR.

"c-TF-IDF" was used (a variant of TF-IDF) to evaluate the importance of each word within each HDBSCAN cluster, selecting representative words for each topic for thematic representation. This algorithm emphasizes keywords with high frequencies within specific classes rather than across all documents, aligning well with the feature extraction requirements of our knowledge graph centered on building health resilience.

C-TF-IDF = TF/sum(TF) * log((N + 1)/(Nc + 1))

Where TF (Term Frequency) represents the frequency of a term within a document, sum(TF) represents the sum of term frequencies within the document, N represents the total number of documents, and Nc represents the number of documents within the cluster or class.

(4) Topic Extraction

By inputting topics and representative documents, prompts are constructed, and the ChatGPT-4o API is utilized to summarize and synthesize the documents, resulting in the final compilation of a topic word library.

Finally, the top-level concepts of BHR are analyzed using a top-down approach, while the bottom-up approach is employed to hierarchically decompose BHR knowledge. The results of these two methods are then integrated and refined through mutual supplementation and matching of concepts and subcategories, culminating in the final BHR ontology.

3.3. LLMs for entity recognition and relation extraction

After constructing the ontology, it is necessary to identify the entities corresponding to the concepts as well as the relations between these entities. However, in an emerging field, extracting entities and relationships from unstructured text using traditional identification methods is complex and requires extensive manual annotation.

LLMs exhibit good performance in text generation and question-answering tasks. There are many LLMs in today's market, among them, ChatGPT is a popular AI tool proposed by OpenAI [63]. ChatGPT operates on a generative artificial intelligence-based question-and-answer system, and it possesses significant abilities in translation, creating stories, debugging code, and other aspects [64]. Particularly regarding summarization capabilities based on text, many scholars have demonstrated that ChatGPT performs well in analyzing and understanding natural language and summarizing the main points according to semantic relations [61,65].

Constructing a knowledge graph from unstructured text is exceedingly complex, requiring extensive manual annotation of entities and relationships. Even semi-automatic supervised algorithms like BI-LSTM-CRF and BERT-BILSTM-CRF for NER need certain annotated data. This process can consume considerable human effort.

Despite some "hallucination", ChatGPT-4o, as a generative AI tool, is highly accredited for its capacity to extract knowledge from unstructured text automatically [66]. ChatGPT-4o excels in text summarization and semantic analysis due to its advanced neural architecture, which enables deeper contextual comprehension, improved coherence, and more accurate extraction of key information. Its ability to understand nuances, identify relationships between concepts, and generate concise yet comprehensive summaries makes it highly effective for tasks requiring precise language processing [67]. Additionally, its enhanced training data and optimization techniques contribute to better generalization across diverse topics, ensuring more relevant and insightful responses.

Using ChatGPT-4o for named entity and relationship recognition not only greatly saves on human annotation efforts but also better understands the contextual semantic relationships in any field's unstructured text. It is not constrained by a specific set of concepts, which is an effective ability that pre-trained NLP models cannot achieve [68].

Utilizing the ChatGPT-4o application programming interface (API) to summarize unstructured knowledge within unstructured texts allows for the refinement and structuring of this knowledge. This article leverages ChatGPT-4o to propose a model for entity and relationship extraction based on feedback checking. The specific content of the model is shown in Fig. 4.

Store the processed multi-modal data in text form and employ prompt engineering to enable ChatGPT to automatically extract triples from the text data. For each text document, ChatGPT follows these steps:

(1) Triple Extraction Prompt Engineering:
    1) Ontology framework learning: Start by inputting the ontology framework. This ensures that the recognized entities and relationships fall within the definitions provided by the ontology.
    2) Annotation case learning: Using three real news articles as examples, manually annotate all triples. These examples will serve as cases for ChatGPT to learn from.
    3) Automated Triples Extraction: ChatGPT automatically extracts triples using the developed prompt engineering strategy. The triples should then be saved in JSON format.

(2) FCM Prompt Engineering: Use the initial triple extraction results alongside the original document as inputs to construct prompts.
    1) Triples supplement: This helps in reprocessing the triples. Begin by comparing the initial triple results with the original document to check for any omissions.
    2) Semantic consistency checking: Verify the relationships between entities and concepts using semantic checks to ensure accuracy.
    3) Deletion of Redundancy: Delete any triples that have the same overall meaning to eliminate redundancy.
    4) Similar Entities Merging: Further, filter the extracted entities by checking their semantic similarity and consistency, and merge the entities with the same semantics.

By following these steps, the process ensures that the extraction and refinement of triples are both systematic and accurate, resulting in a well-structured and reliable triple data set.

3.4. In-context learning prompts

To enhance LLMs' reasoning abilities of entities and relations extraction for BHR, especially, this study employs ICL-based prompts to train the LLMs. ICL has been popularized as a method for learning tasks using LLMs in GPT-3. Essentially, it uses a pre-trained language model to conditionally generate probabilistic distributions based on given examples [69].

In this study, the baseline prompt, few-shot prompt, and Chain of Thought (CoT) prompt are integrated into ChatGPT-4o based on ICL [50], aiming to pre-train ChatGPT-4o's reasoning steps, thereby refining the underlying logic for inferring entity relationship triples of BHR.
