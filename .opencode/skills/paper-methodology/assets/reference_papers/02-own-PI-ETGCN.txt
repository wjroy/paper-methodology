2.	Methodology
Focusing on wall–soil–pipeline deformation responses as key excavation-induced risk responses, this study proposes PI-ETGCN to accurately predict their spatiotemporal co-evolution. Unlike most geotechnical PINN applications that incorporate physics mainly through PDE-residual losses, PI-ETGCN embeds wall–soil–pipeline coupled mechanical priors within a spatiotemporal graph learning architecture. Multi-source construction and monitoring information is integrated as node features, while mechanical priors are encoded as physics-informed adaptive edge features with coupling-consistency constraints between monitoring nodes. This physics–data fusion design effectively captures risk-propagation interactions and enables robust, physically consistent prediction under challenging conditions such as limited samples, noise, and outliers. 
2.1 Overall framework of PI-ETGCN
The overall framework of PI-ETGCN is illustrated in Figure 1, and its mechanical prior basis and developed steps are presented as follows.

(2)	Step 1. (4)	Step 3. Spatiotemporal modeling with PI-ETGCN: With MRGs as input, PI-ETGCN is developed to capture the spatiotemporal evolution of multi-risks. It integrates EGCN and temporal modules for efficient aggregation of node and edge information. Additionally, a physics-informed loss term derived from analytical solutions is incorporated into training objective to constrain the predicted wall–soil deformation coordination patterns, improving prediction stability and physical consistency.
2.2 Theoretical basis for PI-ETGCN development
The core innovation of PI-ETGCN lies in transforming the coupled deformation mechanisms among wall-soil-pipeline systems into explicit physical priors that can be seamlessly embedded within the spatiotemporal modeling framework. This physics-informed formulation enhances interpretability and physical consistency by preventing outputs that violate engineering principles, while improving generalization and robustness under limited, noisy, or distribution-shifted data conditions. This section focuses on modelling the wall–soil–pipeline mechanical coupling within an elastic framework (Figure 2), in which the real layered soil is represented as an equivalent homogeneous, isotropic, linear-elastic half-space under plane strain. The resulting relationships are then formalized as computable expressions to ensure physically consistent model training. For clarity, the main symbols and their explanation used in the physics-informed formulation are summarized in Appendix A.
 
Figure 2. Three-stage mechanical modeling of excavation-induced risk.
(1) Three-stage mechanical modeling of excavation-induced risk
As illustrated in Figure 2 excavation-induced wall–soil–pipeline interactions are represented by a three-stage mechanical process. In Stage 1, excavation unloading causes the retaining wall deflection δw, which drives subsequent soil and pipeline responses. In Stage 2, this wall deflection produces soil loss outside the pit and induces surrounding soil displacements δs. Within the analytical derivation, δs is obtained by superposing the displacement responses of wall segments. The resulting segment-to-soil-point displacement couplings at ground settlement locations later serve as the wall–ground settlement edges in the MRG, and the superposed displacements itself underpins the physics-informed loss. In stage 3, the soil displacements induced by δw generate additional deformation in the vicinity of the pipelines. Idealizing each pipeline as an elastic beam–spring system converts these soil displacements into additional pipeline loads Fp at pipeline monitoring points. The resulting segment-to-pipeline deformation–load couplings establish the wall–pipeline edge features in the MRG. The mechanical modeling and analytical solutions of Stages 2 and 3 are detailed below.
(2) Mechanical modeling of wall–soil interaction in Stage 2
In stage 2, the wall–soil interaction is modeled by an analytical solution for soil displacement induced by wall deflection. Following the analytical theory of Qian et al. [4–6], the soil behind the retaining wall is treated as a plane-strain displacement boundary-value problem. As shown in Figure 3(a), the arbitrary wall-deflection profile is first decomposed into a series of horizontal segments so that the soil displacement induced by δw is viewed as the superposition of the responses of these segments. The soil displacements wi (x, z) induced by wall translation of the i-th segment is obtained from the rigid wall-translation problem, as illustrated in Figure 3(b). This problem is simplified to a plane-strain static case where the wall is prescribed a horizontal displacement and the wall–soil interface is frictionless, while the ground surface is free of surcharge (Figure 3c). By applying mirror symmetry and separation of variables, the soil displacement field is decomposed into two parts and then superposed. 2.3 Multi-source data collection and node-feature integration
The construction of PI-ETGCN requires comprehensive and reliable construction and monitoring data. In this study, multi-source data are extracted from BIM platform and organized into construction-related variables and excavation-induced responses, as summarized in Table 1 and illustrated in Figure 4. The construction-related variables characterize construction disturbances, including excavation and dewatering parameters (Hₑ, Hw) that represent unloading and seepage effects [31]; pit-bottom exposure time (texp) that reflects creep effects [32]; and disturbance descriptors around the pit (vexc, dexc) [33]. The excavation-induced responses include deformation and stress measurements that can be obtained in near real time. The collected time series are preprocessed by missing-value treatment and denoising to ensure temporal continuity, followed by Z-score normalization.
 
Figure 4. Schematic diagram of multi-source construction data and MRG: (a) Cross-section view along the width; (b) Cross-section view along the length.
As illustrated in Figure 4(a), monitoring points along the excavation cross-section are modeled as graph nodes, and the processed multi-source data are assigned as node attributes. The physical relationships among different monitoring points define the graph edges, forming the multi-source risk graph (MRG) in Equation (10). The MRG comprises three groups of local nodes and one global node vG. The local nodes comprise nw wall nodes, ns ground settlement nodes, and np pipeline nodes within the influence zone of the cross-section. In particular, wall nodes are selected from inclinometer measurements along the wall depth, and their spacing is chosen to match the wall discretization in Section 2.2. The total number of local nodes is N = nw + ns + np, which is determined by the monitoring layout. Each local node carries a deformation response scalar δw, δs, or δp. The global node aggregates construction-related variables and auxiliary excavation-induced indicators, including δc and Fs. These auxiliary indicators are not prediction targets, but they indirectly quantify disturbance intensity.

2.4 Adaptive physics-informed edge-feature generation 
To represent the spatial mechanical relationships among risk nodes in the MRG, this section proposes an edge-feature construction scheme that combines analytical solutions with trainable parameters. Based on the wall–soil–pipeline coupling mechanisms described in Section 2.2, element-wise mechanical responses between wall segments and soil or pipeline points are derived and assembled into node-to-node forward edge features. Backward edge features are then generated from the forward features using a logarithmic reciprocal mapping. Trainable parameters are embedded as globally shared calibration factors within the physics-based expressions, preserving interpretability while adapting the analytical couplings to project-specific conditions. The resulting edge-feature matrices are finally processed by doubly stochastic normalization (DSN) to improve numerical stability and facilitate convergence during training.
(1) Wall–ground settlement interaction derived from analytical solution
To obtain element-wise responses, the wall deflection profile is discretized along depth into nw segments whose center depths coincide with the inclinometer depths (Figure 3b). The wall–ground settlement interaction is then described by the settlement at each ground point induced by the translation of each wall segment. Based on the segment-wise formulation in Equation (6), the contribution of the i-th wall segment translation δw,i to the settlement at the j-th ground point δs,ij is obtained from the wall-translation solution by taking the difference between the displacement fields for wall movement down to depths Hi and Hi+1, as expressed in Equation (11). (3) Adaptive edge-feature generation with trainable parameter incorporation
The element-wise responses derived above provide the physical basis for node-to-node edge features. In the MRG, the center of wall segment i is represented by wall node i, and each ground or pipeline monitoring point is represented by a ground node j or pipeline node m. For wall node i, the measured wall deflection δw,i is taken as the segment deflection, and Equations (12)–(13) yield the corresponding settlement δs,ij at ground point j and additional pipeline load Fp,im at pipeline point m. In contrast, Fp,im has units of load. Since the element-wise responses are derived under elastic assumptions, they may not fully capture layered-soil behavior under real field conditions. To enhance adaptability while preserving interpretability, trainable physics parameters (α, β, and γ) are embedded into the analytical expressions via the scaled variables in Equation (14). Specifically, γ rescales the overall displacement amplitude through the wall deflection term [35], α adjusts the horizontal settlement pattern and thus governs the trough width [36], and β has a comparatively smaller effect, mainly fine-tuning the ground settlement trough at z=0 to reflect variations in the settlement influence range with He [12]. To illustrate their effects, a hypothetical case (L=16m and He=8m) is considered with two representative wall-deflection modes adopted from field observations under similar soil conditions [37]. Vertical soil displacements at different depths are then computed for different parameter combinations. As shown in Figure 5, increasing γ amplifies settlement at all depths without changing the overall shape, whereas larger α yields a narrower settlement trough, and β further refines the ground settlement trough. Overall, these parameters provide a physically interpretable adjustments of the analytical solution, enabling the network to learn adaptive physics-informed edge features while retaining the deformation-pattern prior under site-specific soil and support conditions. In addition, backward edge features are introduced to alleviate the underrepresentation of mutual coupling and constrained information flow that arise when only forward edges are used [38,39]. In this setting, backward edges share the same sparsity pattern as forward edges and preserve the forward coupling pattern of wall–soil–pipeline interactions while reweighting their influence in the reverse direction. They act as feedback gains through which monitoring nodes feed information back to the wall in a manner compatible with the underlying mechanics. Meanwhile, the logarithmic form compresses the dynamic range of the reciprocal term and maintains a complementary relation in which weaker forward interactions are compensated by stronger backward feedback.

(4) Edge-feature processing through doubly stochastic normalization
In the PI-ETGCN framework, the matrix constructed from edge features functions as a filter that multiplies the node-feature matrix to update node representations. However, repeated multiplication can gradually amplify or dampen the numerical scale of the edge matrix, potentially compromising training stability and making the model sensitive to the absolute magnitude of physics-informed edges. To mitigate this issue, doubly stochastic normalization (DSN) [40] is applied to preprocess the edge-feature matrix, as defined in Equation (18). After DSN processing, the edge-feature matrix satisfies the canonical properties of a doubly stochastic matrix, including non-negativity, symmetry, semi-positive definiteness, and a constant maximum eigenvalue of 1. These properties control the spectral radius of the propagation operator and stabilize graph convolution, while largely preserving the relative pattern of edge weights across node pairs. As a result, DSN buffers the effect of absolute edge magnitudes and allows the model to focus on deformation-influence patterns, suppressing noisy effects and emphasizing the dominant structural couplings that govern spatiotemporal evolution. Appendix D further examines the bidirectional edge-feature matrices before and after DSN and shows that DSN mainly rescales and mildly smooths edge magnitudes. The dominant spatial pattern and the physically meaningful contrast between near- and far-field interactions are essentially preserved.

2.5 Spatiotemporal evolution modeling with PI-ETGCN
After constructing the MRG dataset, a predictive model is required to capture both data–mechanics coupling and spatiotemporal dependencies embedded in nodes and edges. To this end, this study proposes PI-ETGCN to characterize the spatiotemporal evolution of multi-source risks during deep excavation. As shown in Figure 6, PI-ETGCN takes the historical MRG sequence as input, learns the spatiotemporal mapping function FGCN, and predicts the deformation of each risk point for the next T days using Equation (20). On the spatial level, the EGCN module is incorporated to achieve data–mechanics fusion by jointly aggregating construction-related node features and mechanical coupling information encoded in the edge features. On the temporal level, a GRU-based module is used to capture the dynamic evolution of risk states throughout the construction process. In addition, the analytical solution of wall–soil interaction is incorporated as a physics-based regularization term in the objective function. This forms a hybrid loss that integrates physical constraints with data-driven learning, ensuring that predictions satisfy wall–soil deformation coordination while maintaining robustness under challenging conditions such as small samples, outliers, and high noise. The EGCN module consists of L edge-enhanced convolution layers, with the node update rule defined in Equation (21). Unlike traditional GCNs [41] that rely only on neighboring node features, EGCN explicitly incorporates multi-dimension edge features during graph convolution. This enables the network to capture both topological dependencies and spatial mechanical coupling among wall–soil–pipeline nodes, thereby enhancing physical interpretability and predictive accuracy. Furthermore, because graph convolution in EGCN involves only lightweight edge-weighting operations, it requires significantly fewer trainable parameters than attention-based graph networks (e.g., graph attention network, GAT), achieving lower computational complexity without compromising accuracy.

(2) GRU-based temporal module for sequential dependency modeling
After obtaining spatial node embeddings   that integrate data–physics information, the next step is to model the temporal evolution of risk states. To achieve this, a Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU)–based temporal module is introduced. Unlike conventional recurrent neural networks, GRU employs update and reset gates that effectively capture short-term dependencies while mitigating gradient vanishing and preserving long-term memory. This property makes GRU particularly well-suited for modeling the non-stationary and highly dynamic sequences that characterize risk evolution during deep excavation [19]. At each time step t, the GRU input includes the node embeddings   from the EGCN module and the hidden state ht−1 from the previous step. Its update process is defined by Equations (22)–(25). Through this gated mechanism, the GRU adaptively regulates memory retention and information updating according to the stage of construction. During excavation or support installation, the model emphasizes short-term responses to rapid changes in risk states, whereas during the stable phase following bottom-slab construction, it strengthens long-term dependency learning to capture sustained deformation trends. In comparison with LSTM, GRU adopts a simpler architecture by omitting the explicit forget gate, substantially reducing parameter count and computational cost. Finally, the sequential embeddings output by the GRU H= {Ht-n, Ht-n+1, ..., Ht} are fed into the Multi-Layer Perceptron layer (MLP) to predict future deformations at identified risk points.

(3) Hybrid loss function with physics-informed constraint and evaluation metrics
Relying solely on empirical error minimization may lead to evolution patterns that deviate from mechanical laws, particularly under limited or noisy data conditions. To ensure physically consistent learning, the proposed model adopts a hybrid loss that integrates a data-driven term (LD) with a physics-informed regularization term (LP) derived from the analytical wall–soil coupling model (Section 2.2). This physics-informed term enforces consistency between the predicted deformation patterns of wall deflection and ground settlement, thereby acting as a regularize that improves training stability and enhances generalization. Since Equation (8) pertains to a section perpendicular to the excavation profile (Figure 4a), an analytical pipeline deformation compatible with the profile-based setting cannot be constructed. The wall–pipeline interaction is therefore indirectly constrained through the same physics-informed regularization acting on the coupled graph representation.
The physics-informed loss is formulated from the analytical soil displacement under arbitrary wall deflections (Equations 6–7). To adapt this representation for graph modeling, Equation (6) is rewritten using the edge-feature definition in Equation (15), so that ground settlements are computed jointly from wall-node predictions and the edge-feature matrix (Equation 26), and the associated physics term is defined in Equation (27). As the analytical solution relies on simplified assumptions and may deviate under complex field conditions, this term is treated as a soft constraint. To reduce sensitivity to absolute magnitudes implied by the analytical solution, both analytical and predicted settlements are spatially max-normalized by their respective maximum absolute values over soil nodes within the analytical influence zone. This normalization emphasizes deformation-pattern consistency and keeps LP numerically well-scaled for joint optimization with LD. The hybrid loss then combines LD and LP with a weighting coefficient λ, as shown in Equation (29). With LP internally normalized to a dimensionless and comparable scale, λ is set to 1 as a default weighting in this study. This setting treats the analytical term as a soft regularizer rather than a dominant constraint, which helps limit the influence of analytical bias and avoids introducing an additional tuning parameter during training.
