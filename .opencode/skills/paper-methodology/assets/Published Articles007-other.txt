2. Method
2.1. Framework of the proposed TSFRA
2.1.1. Problem definition
This study aims to conduct a risk assessment focusing on evaluating
the impact of excavation activities on adjacent buildings across various
stages and locations. The assessment relies on the information fusion
from expert judgment and historical settlement data. To accomplish this
objective, a monitoring system equipped with numerous sensors is
deployed along the perimeter of the adjacent buildings to continuously
monitor and record real-time cumulative settlement. Each sensor is
strategically located and designated as a monitoring point (identified by
labels such as JFxx, for example, JF42-JF85). While the building is
influenced by the long-term effects of excavation activities at a nearby
foundation pit, the construction process is divided into different construction conditions (denoted as ax, e.g., A1-A7), following the chronological order of the excavation stages.
2.1.2. Overview
To comprehensively assess the impact of excavation-induced risk on
the surrounding buildings, the TSFRA model incorporating FAHP,
DGDT, and CM is developed. The architecture of TSFRA is shown in
Fig. 1, which is made up of four key phases to form a systematic decision
support tool.
(1) Multi-sensor information collection: Sensors are strategically
positioned around the targeted building to automatically and
periodically record ground settlement at various monitoring
points, reflecting the risk status influenced by excavation. Then,
these data can be reasonably cropped based on the progression
sequence of excavation conditions for deeper investigations.
(2) Temporal weight determination with FAHP: In the temporal
dimension, excavation work exerts dynamic effects on the risk
status of surrounding structures over time. With the assistance of
FAHP, several experts in underground engineering are invited to
apply triangular fuzzy numbers to quantify fuzzy weights associated with structures at key stages.
(3) Spatial weight determination with DGDT: In the spatial
dimension, excavation-induced risk varies across monitoring locations due to factors like relative positions and geological conditions. DGDT translates spatial risk assessment into causal graph
structuring, with DAG-GNN to uncover causality relationships
among multiple monitoring points under different excavation
conditions. Then, as the output of DAG-GNN, a causal graph is fed
into DEMATEL and topological analysis to compute spatial
weights, identifying primary sources of risk.
(4) Information fusion-based risk assessment with CM: To further
fuse temporal and spatial information derived from FAHP and
DGDT, CM is performed to convert qualitative and quantitative
concepts and establish spatial cloud models. Weights are assigned
based on spatial weights, and temporal clouds are generated
through product operation. The targeted cloud combines temporal clouds with temporal weights, introducing randomness to
reflect uncertainty in risk assessment.
2.2. Temporal weight determination with FAHP
During foundation pit construction, the evolution of risks to surrounding buildings across different excavation conditions is relatively
complex. In this section, FAHP aims to grasp the risk status at key
excavation conditions from expert insights based on the additive
weighting process, especially in the presence of uncertainties and conflicting information. As an extension of the traditional AHP, FAHP builds
upon a robust foundation of fuzzy mathematics and employs fuzzy sets
to quantify the relative relationships among various influential factors,
and thus it can successfully mitigate the issue of information loss
inherent in AHP. Clearly, FAHP can decompose a decision-making
problem into a hierarchy following four basic steps, including the
establishment of the fuzzy pairwise comparison matrix, aggregation of
expert judgments, calculation of the fuzzy weights of the criteria,
defuzzification of the fuzzy weights, and measurement of the consistency for limited contradiction. The specific steps are as follows:

2.3. Spatial weight determination with DGDT
To obtain the settlement risk distribution across monitoring points, a
comprehensive weight calculation method called DGDT is developed by
integrating deep neural networks, matrix theory, and graph theory,
resembling causality-based network analysis. As shown in Fig. 3, DGDT
comprises three key components: DAG-GNN, the DEMATEL method, and
topological analysis. First, DAG-GNN extracts temporal causal relationships among time-series data from multiple monitoring points of
adjacent buildings, emphasizing the impact of one point on another and
highlighting data interconnectedness. The resulting adjacency matrix is
then used to identify critical factors through DEMATEL and topological
analysis. DEMATEL determines each point’s centrality, while topological analysis identifies key nodes in the causal graph using PageRank and
clustering coefficients. Finally, the weighted sum of these three factors
determines the spatial weights, reflecting the comprehensive importance ranking of each monitoring point. The specific steps of DGDT are
as follows:
Step 1: DAG-GNN model establishment
DAG-GNN belongs to a deep learning algorithm specially designed
for causal inference on multivariate time series data. To be more specific, DAG is a commonly used graphical structure within a Bayesian
network to represent causal relationships between variables, while GNN
with Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) can operate within DAG. By feeding
the settlement data reflecting excavation-induced risk from multiple
monitoring points, DAG-GNN can aggregate and transmit information
while updating weights and minimizing the loss function to learn the
most accurate way of information propagation aligned with the genuine
causal mechanism.
As shown in Fig. 4, the architecture of DAG-GNN is in an encoder decoder framework. More specifically, MLP learns the information
from the original data within the encoder to construct the posterior
distribution of latent variables. Utilizing reparameterization, it stochastically samples latent variables, which are then reconstructed
within the decoder to produce the original data. During training, DAGGNN can continually minimize the reconstruction error between the
original data and generated data. This process involves backpropagation
to update network parameters, which helps unveil the most plausible
causal mechanisms to guide data generation and reveal causal relationships among multiple variables.

updated to minimize L c(A,W,λ). In Eq. (9), h(A)→0 drives the model to
go convergence. Eventually, it outputs the weighted adjacency matrix

Step 3: Topological analysis
To address DEMATEL’s limitations, topological analysis is carried
out to offer decision-makers an intuitive visualization of the excavation
system’s structure. Relying on the graphic, nodes in the structure
represent monitoring points with time-series data, and connections
signify causal relationships revealed by DAG-GNN. This analysis uncovers intricate relationships within the system, shedding light on the
dynamic nature of excavation-related risks. For dynamic systems, topological analysis at the microscopic level can also help to investigate
the temporal evolution process of networks by tracking the changes in
nodes and connections over time.
Herein, metrics such as clustering coefficient and PageRank in Eqs.
(13) and (14) are utilized for micro-level analysis, indicating densely
connected clusters and node influence, respectively. Higher values of
these metrics may indicate localized structural instability or potential
vulnerabilities in the excavation system.

2.4. Information fusion-based risk assessment with CM
As a fuzzy mathematical technique, CM is employed to efficiently
fuse information from various sources, including temporal weights from
FAHP, spatial weights from DGDT, and monitoring data. That is to say,
CM can create a multidimensional and comprehensive information
system, allowing for achieving a final comprehensive risk assessment
under great uncertainties with increased accuracy and reduced bias. To
be more specific, the numerical features of CM include the expectation
(Ex), entropy (En), and hyper-entropy (He). Among them, Ex reflects the
centroid position of all cloud droplets in spatial distribution. En reveals
the correlation between the randomness and fuzziness of the cloud
droplet distribution, which is reflected in the dispersion of all cloud
droplets. He measures the degree of uncertainty of En, which is reflected
in the thickness of all cloud droplets. For an intuitive understanding,
Fig. 5 shows a cloud model composed of 3000 cloud droplets with numerical eigenvalues (Ex, En, He) = (0, 0.3, 0.02).
Herein, the information fusion process by CM is in a hierarchical
structure, which is made up of three layers, including the target layer
(building risks), temporal layer (excavation conditions), and spatial
layer (multiple monitoring points). The final results are presented in the
form of a fuzzy cloud to better handle the uncertainty and fuzziness of
risks, and thus each risk level can be expressed by cloud representations.
This can assist decision-makers in better understanding the distribution
of risks under different temporal and spatial conditions. The specific
steps are as follows:
